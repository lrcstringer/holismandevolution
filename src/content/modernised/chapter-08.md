title: "Darwinism and Holism"
chapter: 8

Summary: Darwin’s idea of Organic Descent (the theory that all species are descended from earlier forms through gradual modification) and the laws he formulated to explain it began one of the most far-reaching revolutions in human thought. Holism (the view that wholes are more than the sum of their parts and act as real causal agents) offers a fresh way of looking at the Darwinian factors, and pushes the scope of Evolution beyond the strictly biological realm. Darwin traced Organic Descent to the interwoven action of two factors: an inner creative factor — Variation — operating spontaneously and somewhat mysteriously inside organisms and altering their inherited structures and functions in very small degrees; and an external factor — Natural Selection — which sifts these slight variations, weeding out the organisms whose variations were less suited to their environment, and leaving organisms with suitable variations free to multiply and develop. Through the continuous accumulation of small useful variations across many generations, definite specific characters (the distinctive traits that mark out a species) would eventually be established, and new species would arise.

Darwin placed the most weight on Natural Selection. On Variation he was vague and hesitant, but there is little doubt that he counted both inborn variations and individually acquired modifications among the elements that ultimately become specific characters. In this way, the countless forms of life would in the end be shaped by both factors into very close conformity and adaptation to their conditions of life.

Darwin’s grand conception has been somewhat blurred by later developments, in which attention has shifted from Natural Selection to Variation. First, Weismann denied that acquired characters can be passed on, which made it hard to understand how organisms could gradually become fitted and adapted to their environment through experience and habit. Then De Vries dropped small variations from the picture altogether and attributed all advance to large, well-marked “mutations” (sudden, inheritable jumps in form) occurring only occasionally. This made slow age-long adaptation — for instance, to particular habitats and ecological conditions — even harder to account for. Finally, the Mendelians or Geneticists developed the view that organisms contain well-marked, stable unit-characters whose combinations in crossing follow a definite law; experimental Evolution and Genetics today consist mostly in tracing these unit-characters and manipulating them in breeding and in the laboratory. The idea of more or less mechanical combinations has thus replaced the idea of creative variations that lay at the heart of the Darwinian conception. As a result, it becomes very difficult to understand how new variation arises at all, and how Evolution is genuinely progressive and creative rather than just a more or less stationary reshuffling of existing characters.

These later developments take too narrow a view of Evolution as a whole, and so tend to become one-sided and to overemphasise certain aspects of the total process. They are right, however, in stressing the inner creative factor, which is the real positive driving force of Evolution. The real secret lies in the cell — in the germ-cell or fertilised ovum — rather than in the external situation, important as that is.

There is, however, no doubt that variation is influenced indirectly by external ecological conditions, which show up, for instance, in the general characters of plant formations and communities. And there can likewise be little doubt that acquired characters do, in the long run, reach down to the hereditary germ-cell and become transmissible variations. While these variations are still small and have no survival value, the acquired characters and the routines of animal life shield and nurse them until they are strong and developed enough to confer survival value on their organisms. Modifications (changes acquired during an individual’s life) are therefore the rough material out of which variations are made; and in that sense Weismann was wrong, while Darwin — and further back, even Lamarck — was right.

There is, however, a further complication that cannot be handled on purely Darwinian principles. Modifications and variations do not arrive singly but in clusters, involving many minor and consequential modifications and variations. Are all of these “selected” individually, even before they have any survival value or strength? These difficulties force us to look deeper, to abandon the idea that variations are picked off one by one, and to see the advance not as that of a single variation or set of variations but of the organism as a whole. It is the organism that advances along a more or less limited front; the “variation” is only the most conspicuous point of advance, but there is a whole curve of advance involving many other minor points. In other words, the advance is holistic, and the variation is only the most striking item in a whole series. The progress and survival of the variation are an equally holistic matter. The organism is simply maintaining its own forward movement in the variation; the variation issues from it and is in line with its whole trend and direction; the variation is not isolated and unsupported — behind it stands the whole force of the organism, of whose inner movement the variation is merely the most tangible expression. It is thus the organism as a whole which, in the first instance, “selects” the winning variation or series, and confers on it support and survival value. “Holistic Selection” is therefore at work at the birth of the variation and through its early nursing stage, and it is only once the variation has matured that Natural Selection takes over and the variation begins to fend for itself, so to speak.

Holism must likewise be invoked to explain organic co-ordination. Without it, for instance, it is impossible to give a satisfactory account of the countless co-ordinations and co-adaptations of structure and function that make up the action of a living organism. No purely mechanical explanation of co-ordinated animal movement or action has ever been provided. The animal acts as a whole, with a unity and effectiveness of action that is not just the mechanical sum of its movements. Holism — not merely as a concept but as a real feature of nature — is necessary to account for this unique unity of organic or psychic action.

Holism includes not only variations but, just as much, the repression of variations. It is as often inhibitive as it is creative. Certain features are held in check while others are released and pushed forward in organic evolution, and in this way the balanced whole of the Type (the characteristic form of a species or kind) is achieved. Darwinism neglects this repressive aspect of progress, but it is just as real as active variation. Together, the two underlie the types and structures of life. This repressive tendency, already fully at work on the organic level, becomes much more conspicuous on the psychical level, where it operates as the ethical restraint that is so essential in the formation of Personality as a moral whole.

From the holistic point of view, it can be shown that the inner and outer factors in Evolution lie much closer together than is commonly thought, and the grandeur of the Darwinian vision, far from being dimmed, stands out in even greater fullness.

Finally, Beauty in Nature is holistic — it belongs to the whole, comes from Holism, and can be explained on no other principle. Holism as a theory therefore accounts not only for the origin of forms and types but also for their Values, which far transcend the survival values needed merely for Nature’s utilitarian purposes.

NEWTON’S Law of Gravitation is perhaps the most striking instance in the whole history of science of one simple generalisation gathering up an enormous range of physical facts within its sweep. By the time the law was formulated, the new heliocentric (sun-centred) view of the universe had already become widely accepted, but vast masses of facts remained that had not yet been brought into a single framework, and that called for explanation from the new point of view. Newton’s concept of gravitation, with its law of the Inverse Square (the gravitational force between two bodies decreases in proportion to the square of the distance between them), proved completely effective. The behaviour of falling bodies on this earth, the motions of objects on its surface, the movements of the solar system and of the starry universe as a whole, and many of the phenomena of physics as known and understood at the time — all seemed to find their proper place and explanation under this all-embracing formula.

Newton did not pretend to understand or explain gravitation itself, and his lifelong meditations on this profound problem gave him no clue as to its nature. But he formulated the law of its action — what happens on the assumption that gravitation operates — with such simplicity and effectiveness that it became another instance of Columbus’ egg (a problem that seems impossible until someone shows the obvious solution, after which it appears trivial). As is the way with all human matters, the law itself eventually came to be regarded as more than a law — as an explanation, indeed as an operative factor itself accounting for all the phenomena it covered. It is only in our own day that gravitation in this sense has been overthrown, and Newton’s law has come to have a restricted application. Relativity has dethroned gravitation, and for the moment Einstein’s Ten Equations rule the universe, where before the inverse-square equation was the only and unquestioned code.

Immanuel Kant, himself one of the great kings and lawgivers of thought, regarded the Newtonian system as final; he envisioned some future Newton who would discover and formulate the laws of life, just as Newton had laid down the laws of motion and matter. Beyond all doubt, Darwin fulfilled that vision — not perhaps in the sense Kant intended, but in a way that has made him perhaps an even more epoch-making figure than Newton. Newton was epoch-making for science, while Darwin has been epoch-making in a far more fundamental sense. He has changed our whole human orientation in knowledge and belief; he has given a new direction to our outlook, our efforts and our aspirations; and he has probably made a greater difference to human thought and action than any other single thinker. But even he is not the last word.

Darwin is even less the last word than Newton. He has pointed out the great way, and on that way we are already travelling beyond his great vision.

Let me first state Darwin’s law, which was as simple as Newton’s and far easier to grasp. Among living things there is a tendency to vary and to over-multiply; as a result, a struggle for survival becomes inevitable, and in this struggle for existence the fittest survive. This explains the origin of species and all the organic differences in the world. The tendency to variation is a fact obvious to everyone; so is the over-multiplication of individuals when conditions are favourable and external restraints are absent; and the resulting struggle, which Darwin calls Natural Selection, is well known to anyone with even the slightest knowledge of living Nature. These are the simple bricks of fact out of which the Darwinian theory is built. It is surely as striking an example of Columbus’ egg as has ever been presented. The genius of the Master was shown by the vastness of the structure he raised on these simple concepts of common sense and ordinary experience. From these commonplace facts he explained the infinite variety of life-forms on earth, their geographical distribution both past and present, and the marvellously close adaptation of species to the physical and other conditions among which they live — adaptation to land and sea, to fresh and salt water, to soil and climate ranging from extreme heat to extreme cold, to the widest range of wet, arid and desert conditions, and to all the countless facts and situations that go into the weaving of the mysterious web of life.

The whole Darwinian theory is summarised in the closing sentences of the Origin of Species with a simplicity and beauty of statement worthy of the simple yet profound genius of the Master, and in just a few touches they raise before us the great Darwinian vision. They have often been quoted, but they will bear quoting again here, and for all time:

“It is interesting to contemplate a tangled bank, clothed with many plants of many kinds, with birds singing in the bushes, with various insects flitting about, and with worms crawling through the damp earth — and to reflect that these elaborately constructed forms, so different from one another, and dependent on each other in such a complex way, have all been produced by laws acting around us. These laws, taken in the broadest sense, are: Growth coupled with Reproduction; Inheritance, which is almost implied by reproduction; Variability, arising from the indirect and direct action of the conditions of life and from use and disuse; a Rate of Increase so high as to lead to a struggle for life, and as a consequence to Natural Selection, which entails Divergence of Character (the splitting of a lineage into more strongly differing forms) and the extinction of less-improved forms. Thus, from the war of Nature, from famine and death, the most exalted object we are capable of conceiving — namely, the production of the higher animals — directly follows. There is grandeur in this view of life, with its several powers, having been originally breathed by the Creator into a few forms or into one; and that, while this planet has gone cycling on according to the fixed law of gravity, from so simple a beginning, endless forms most beautiful and most wonderful have been, and are being, evolved.”

I will freely admit that there are few passages in the great literature of the world that move me more deeply than these closing words of Darwin’s great book. They have a force and a beauty out of all proportion to their simple, unadorned phrasing. They are the expression of a great selfless soul, who sought truth utterly and fearlessly, and was in the end granted (vouchsafed) a vision of the unity of life that has perhaps never been surpassed in fullness and grandeur. Darwin assumed two operative factors in the organic world: (1) Variation in the reproduction and inheritance of living beings, and (2) Natural Selection, or the survival of the fittest, as Herbert Spencer called it. Darwin’s name is principally linked with the second factor, with which his works mostly deal and which he elaborated with an unrivalled wealth of detail. He gave much less attention to Variation, and indeed used it mainly as a peg on which to hang his theory of the origin of species through Natural Selection. Variation was, for him, a mysterious fact for Natural Selection to work on. But its spontaneous, uncontrolled character puzzled him. He found no helpful imagery to explain the puzzle. He proposed the theory of Pangenesis (the hypothesis that every part of the body sheds tiny particles, “gemmules,” that gather in the reproductive cells and so transmit acquired traits), which showed great insight, but his successors have not adopted it. The germ-cell theory, which provided a mechanism for both heredity and variation, was a later discovery. The science of Genetics has mainly arisen since his day. Not only were his views on variation meagre and vague, but even the views he did hold have not been adopted by later Darwinians. Thus, in the passage just quoted, he attributes variation to the “direct and indirect action of the conditions of life, and to use and disuse.” Most Darwinians today hold strongly opposing views and deny that these are the sources of Variation. At present there seem to be signs of a backlash (abreaction), of a return to Darwin and even to Lamarck, and of a tendency to look more favourably on Darwin’s views on this important point. But the fact remains that Darwin is on the whole vague on the subject of Variation, and concentrated all his strength on the other principle of Natural Selection and its effects in shaping the organic world.

However that may be, there is no doubt that both Variation and Natural Selection are essential elements in the Darwinian theory. Darwinism, in fact, implies two factors: an internal factor operating mysteriously in the inmost nature and constitution of living organisms, and an external factor working along independent lines on the results achieved by the internal one. The inner factor, Variation, is positive and creative, producing all the variations that serve as raw material for progress. The external factor, Natural Selection, is essentially negative and destructive, eliminating the harmful or less fit variations and leaving the more fit or useful ones free to continue and multiply, and in the process fitting and adapting the individual to the character of its environment. As De Vries has phrased it, the inner factor explains the arrival, and the external factor the survival, of the fit or useful variation or organism.

Darwin’s over-emphasis on the second, external factor had one very unfortunate result: it directly and powerfully reinforced and exaggerated the mechanistic view of the universe. The vera causa (true cause) of organic change and progress appeared to be Natural Selection — an external factor operating on organisms ab extra (from outside), in the same way that physical or dynamical forces are imposed on bodies or their parts from without. Mechanical analogies began to be applied, and Evolution came to be regarded as the mechanics of organic development — Entwicklungsmechanik (developmental mechanics), the term popularised by Wilhelm Roux. The whole tendency of Darwinism has therefore been to greatly strengthen the dominance of the mechanistic hypothesis, which through Darwinism has come to extend its sway from the kingdom of matter into the kingdom of life. What is more, the simplicity of the Darwinian theory has helped to make not only Evolution, but the mechanical view of Evolution, common property. The mystery of progress seemed to become quite simple and intelligible on this theory. It all came down to the survival of the fittest, and the survival of the fittest was so simple and clear an idea — and one so deeply rooted in our ordinary empirical experience — that it seemed a matter of course, which had only to be pointed out by Darwin to be accepted by everybody. The difficult part of the theory — the aspect that had remained a mystery even to Darwin, namely the inner creative factor of Variation — was ignored while Darwinism was being generally accepted, and accepted in the mechanical sense.

This was the first phase of Darwinism, the phase in which Natural Selection was chiefly stressed and was the dominant note in the theory. Then came the second phase, when attention began to be given to the other factor, Variation. With this Neo-Darwinian phase the name of Weismann is forever honourably associated. Weismann turned the gaze of evolutionists from the outside to the inside of the process — from the apparent mechanism of external interaction and clash to the mystery of the inner process. And what he taught was not only most surprising but, in its final Mendelian form, remains one of the most significant and important truths in the whole range of biology. I shall deal with this matter shortly. But before doing so I want to point out that Weismann and his fellow workers were handicapped in their efforts by the mechanical view of Evolution that had already become a fixed dogma in the earlier stage of Darwinism. If anywhere, the mechanistic conception ought to have received its quietus (final blow, death) in the domain of Variation — in the exploration of the inner process or factor of Evolution. Unfortunately, Weismann and several of the most prominent biologists who developed this second phase of Darwinism took up their task not only as convinced Darwinians but as mechanistic Darwinians.

The great battle in which Darwinism had won was tacitly considered a victory for the mechanical view of it. As a result, the whole problem of Variation, as these leading Neo-Darwinians saw it, became one of investigating or finding the mechanism of Variation. Their services have been great, and the route they have opened up will in years to come lead to even greater results. But there is no doubt that the mechanistic view has been a serious handicap to them, and that many of their errors are directly traceable to its disturbing and distorting influence.

The root of Weismann’s difficulties lies in his mechanistic conception of the germ-cell. The cell, as we saw in Chapter IV, already shows in its metabolism many of the functions and activities we associate with the complete individual organism. It is itself a holistic individual, with the most marvellous selective and regulative powers — reminding us, on a much lower plane, of what at a later stage of Evolution appears as the psychical factor (the mental or mind-related dimension). This applies even more to the germ-cell than to ordinary body-cells. Here the concept of the “field” is especially important. Experimental evolutionists look for more in the physical elements of the germ-cell than is actually there. There is, for example, more in the heredity of the germ-cell than can be identified by an analysis of its physical elements. And this more lies in its “field” — that part of the germ-cell which has not yet crystallised and hardened into observable structure. The organic field, as explained in a previous chapter, is the milieu (surrounding medium) of interwoven influences, of internal and external stimuli and responses, in and around the cell or organism. The functioning of the cell or organism as a whole depends on this milieu just as much as on the individual elements of its structure. Much of its past and its future is in its field; in its field the creative adjustments are begun that are eventually translated into and incorporated into its structure. Here as elsewhere, the field is the area of becoming, of creativeness, the growing surface of the structure. To restrict our view of the germ-cell to its identifiable elements of structure is simply to atomise our conceptions on chemical analogies, and to narrow them unduly — neglecting very important features in the germ-cell’s functions and activities, and forcing us in the end to adopt that mechanical view which is the negation of its inmost nature as a living, holistic, individual unity. In this chapter I shall try to show how the concept of Holism dissolves the difficulties created by the mechanistic conception of Variation.

The alterations introduced into the Darwinian scheme by Darwin’s successors have had a profound effect on that scheme as a whole — so much so that today it is hard to say how much of Darwin’s great vision still survives. To see this clearly, it is best to compare Darwin’s general ideas about the facts of Variation with the modifications introduced by his successors.

In Darwin’s view, it was not only the operation of Natural Selection that was moulding living things to fit their environment by eliminating those less suited to it. Variation was also doing its share in assimilating organisms to and adapting them to their environment. The close fit of species to their habitats and environmental conditions, which is so distinctive of living Nature, was, according to him, the combined effect of both Natural Selection and Variation.

For the sake of clarity in what follows, we have to distinguish between various forms of so-called “variation” in living things. In the first place, we have modifications, which arise from the functional activities and experiences of the individual during its own lifetime, and not from inheritance from parents or ancestors. The effects on the bodily organism, or on particular organs, of their use or disuse in some definite way are examples of such modifications. An animal changes its mode of life and as a result stops using certain organs, or begins to use them in a new way or for a new purpose. Such disuse tends to lead to atrophy (wasting away) of these organs, just as new or increased use would develop them. Such atrophy or development in the bodily organism is a modification. All changes or characteristics acquired during the individual’s life are modifications.

In the second place we have variations, which are small inborn changes that pass on through inheritance and are not due to anything the individual develops or acquires during its own life. A small alteration from the type that an animal has inherited from its parents is a variation, in contrast to a modification, which is brought about within its own lifetime. In the third place, a large, very marked inborn change is called a mutation. Any inborn change large and marked enough to constitute a new variety or species is a mutation.

Now I think it is beyond question that, on Darwin’s view, all three forms of change — modifications, variations and mutations — were useful and operative in the eventual production of new species. Modifications due to individual use or disuse he certainly pressed into the service of his scheme of Evolution; and although it is not entirely clear how far other modifications were similarly used by him, it follows from the passage quoted above, as well as from other passages in his works, that variations due to “the indirect or direct action of the conditions of life” — in other words, alterations affecting the individual’s life — could, to an extent he never clearly defined, contribute to the production of new species. As for mutations, while he gave reasons for not believing that great and sudden changes are the ordinary rule of Evolution, he certainly cannot be said to have excluded them. His view was that the slow, gradual summation of small fluctuations and variations, continuously preserved or kept going by Natural Selection, would — and in fact did — over the course of many generations amount to a change large and marked enough to constitute a new type or species. The continuous summation of the effects of use and disuse and the other conditions of life, together with the accidental inborn variations of more mysterious origin, would necessarily co-operate with Natural Selection in producing the close adaptation of species to environment. The result was the vast and intricate system of adaptations and co-adaptations, of harmonious adjustment between Nature and organic life, branching out through the infinite details of the web of life that we see around us. Thus Evolution was explained; thus all the fine adjustments and adaptations in Nature were explained. The only requirement was that a very long time be available for the infinitesimal calculus (the cumulative working out of countless tiny effects) of Natural Selection to produce specific results, and the astronomers and geologists granted this requirement. Darwin’s view seemed to fit very well with the fossil record and with the facts of geographical distribution, which he considered the keystone to the laws of life. No wonder Darwin’s theory proved irresistible and crushed all the older points of view. The triumph of his splendid vision of Evolution seemed complete.

Then the second phase of Darwinism began, with a detailed search for the methods and mechanism of Variation, and with the venue shifted from the wide range of Nature to the research laboratory of Genetics. First, Weismann ruled out the inheritance of acquired characters, and of modifications due to use, disuse, or other environmental conditions acting on the individual life. Only the accidental germinal variations — and none of the moulding effects of the environment on the individual — could contribute to the building up of new species. Then De Vries came forward and largely eliminated small variations from the account, effectively confining progress to mutations. Finally, the experimental Mendelians or Geneticists arrived, and through their researches and experiments appeared to confine Evolution to the interchange, combinations and permutations of definite, already-existing unit characters. The combined effect of these three advances on the Darwinian theory might appear to be largely destructive of Darwinism itself. If, with the Mendelians, we hold that the interchange of definite pre-existing unit characters is all there is to Evolution, real advance becomes impossible and creative Evolution disappears. If, with De Vries, accidental mutation is largely all that Natural Selection has to work on, then advance becomes a most precarious affair, instead of the steady, continuous, delicate process that has been going on through the geological ages. If, with Weismann, modifications from use and disuse and similar causes have no survival value and play no part in the formation of new species, it becomes very difficult to understand the universal close-fitting adaptations of species to their conditions of life. For there is nothing in common between accidental variations and Natural Selection, and there is no clear reason why this clash should not produce chaos and disaster, rather than the harmonies and adjustments that actually characterise the relations of living and non-living Nature. Darwin’s theory, even if wrong in its details, certainly served to explain and make intelligible the broad facts of order, adjustment and progress observable in living Nature. His successors’ theories, even where correct in detail, fail to explain these facts, and turn the world of life as a whole into something unintelligible and in some respects incredible.

It would, however, be a serious mistake to regard the more recent developments in the science of Genetics as covering the whole wide field of the Darwinian theory. So far as I know, they have no such scope, nor are they so intended or understood by those responsible for the very important Genetics research now being successfully carried out in biological laboratories. These researches are intended to follow up a special line first opened by the experiments of the Abbot Mendel of Brünn in Darwin’s own time. They occupy a very restricted area within the whole field of organic Evolution, and are really only concerned with sorting out the special set of problems that arise when races, varieties or definitely distinct variations are crossed or hybridised. Those problems centre on the important question of how biological characters that already exist — whether visible (patent) or hidden (masked) — behave when brought into contact with each other. Mendel found that certain existing characters behave as firm and stable units, very much as atoms or molecules do in chemical combination, and he also discovered the law of the proportions in which these unit characters are reproduced in the offspring. Thus, if individuals carrying dominant character a are crossed with individuals carrying recessive character b, then in the second filial generation the numbers of pure a, pure b, and mixed a-and-b individuals are given by the algebraic formula (a + b)² = a² + 2ab + b². In other words, 25 per cent of the second generation will be pure a’s, 25 per cent pure b’s, and 50 per cent will be individuals with mixed qualities, which on being crossed again with each other will once more produce pure a’s and b’s and mixed ab’s in the same algebraic proportions, and so on apparently ad infinitum (without end). His findings have been amply confirmed by later inquiries, which have also established that not only do these unit characters behave as fixed and stable entities, but — much like radicle groups (atomic groupings that act as a single unit) in Chemistry — groups of such unit characters also sometimes behave as stable combinations and combine with other unit characters as persistent unities. This is all very remarkable and interesting, with important bearings on the practical improvement of breeds and races of animals, and on the beginnings of the new science of Eugenics. But for our present purpose it is enough to point out that Mendelism or Genetics deals with the manipulation of existing characters, not with their origin, genesis or creation. The main question facing organic Evolution — how specific characters that have not existed before are produced — is not directly touched by Mendelism. The problem of the creativeness of Evolution, in the origin of species and in organic progress generally, lies beyond Mendelism’s province. Mendelism deals with the results already achieved by Evolution, not with the creative process by which they were achieved. No doubt it may, and in due course will, incidentally throw important sidelights on this mysterious creative process; but they will probably be no more than sidelights. In other words, Mendelism is not the real method or path of organic Evolution, but at best only an important side-track. This is not intended as a slight on the science of Genetics, but only to place it in its proper perspective within the whole field of organic Evolution.

Having ruled out Mendelism, can we accept De Vries’ Mutation as the ordinary method of Creative Evolution? Mutation occurs when specific or varietal characters appear, not as the result of a slow age-long summation of small variations, but at one bound — with a great leap from one generation to the next. An individual of species X produces offspring that constitute a stable variety or a new species. De Vries observed this happening in the case of the cultivated Œnothera lamarckiana (a species of evening-primrose) growing wild in a potato-field at Hilversum in Holland. Other instances have been observed by other researchers. It has been objected that De Vries’ Œnothera was a cultivated artificial hybrid, with mixed qualities like the ab’s of the Mendelians, and that all he observed was the emergence of pure qualities from this mixture — in other words, not the appearance of new characters but the unmasking of concealed or latent characters that already existed. Other criticisms have also been made of the Mutation theory, which we need not consider here. Despite these criticisms, it is practically certain that mutations do occur in the course of Evolution. But while they almost certainly happen on occasion, they are not common, and do not constitute the ordinary method of organic Evolution. On rare occasions there is a saltus (a leap, a discontinuous jump) — a creative leap forward from one generation to another. A species that has long balanced itself precariously on the edge of a great change suddenly makes the jump, secures a foothold on the other side, and marks the beginning of a new variety or species. But this can at best be only an exceptional, if not rare, effort on Nature’s part. These sudden long jumps can only be very occasional, not the normal procedure in the origin of species. Otherwise we would certainly see more of them, and they would not be the subject of doubt or dispute. The rarity of their observation points to the rarity of their occurrence. And they must be largely confined to cultivated artificial species or varieties, which are more unstable and more violently variable than natural species or varieties. In wild nature, mutation is an occasional and exceptional occurrence, not the ordinary procedure of Evolution.

Footnote: Professor J. P. Lotsy, who bases Evolution on hybridisation between varieties and species, and who has surveyed the flora of several countries from this point of view, has concluded that hybrids are by no means uncommon in Nature, and that a fair percentage of natural species, ordinarily classified as such, are really hybrids. Both Mendelian and experimental species — such as those that T. H. Morgan bred in the fruit-fly Drosophila — are held by Lotsy to be nothing but hybrids.

On the views currently prevailing, the chromosomes of the dividing nucleus have been identified as the main carriers of an organism’s hereditary characters; these characters have been correlated to some extent with distinct chromosomes, and so the number, shape, size and other differences of chromosomes in the germ-cell nucleus are taken to be the physical basis of the characters that distinguish species. It has been found necessary to go further and to suppose that, within the chromosomes themselves, there are active elements or factors — called genes — that produce organic characters. These researches and speculations are still in their initial stages, but they are important and have this advantage: the results of intercrossing and hybridising in producing changes of character can be studied alongside the simultaneous changes in the number and form of chromosomes. In experimental Evolution, the parallelism between cell structure and variation in organic characters thus provides a double weapon of attack. The phenotype (the organism’s observable traits) and the genotype (its underlying genetic make-up) can be studied together.

But the question still remains how this mechanism of heredity operates as the sole and independent cause of Variation. The intermixture of chromosomes from two separate individuals in sexual reproduction, and the changes in the chromosome content of the reproductive cells in their previous meiotic division (the special cell division that halves the chromosome number to produce gametes), undoubtedly create the occasion for a great mixing of parental elements, and are therefore potent sources of Variation. But Variation operates even apart from, and in the absence of, sexual reproduction and the related meiotic divisions of the germ-cells. And the question remains whether the individual life is, in fact, so isolated from the germ-cell that it has no influence on it or on the resulting offspring. Most biologists are particularly insistent on this isolation. The principle of the non-transmissibility of organic modifications (as defined above) is a fixed dogma, based both on the gene theory and on empirical experience, since no clear and indisputable case of such individual modifications being passed on to offspring has been recorded or observed.

There can, however, be little doubt that in adopting this extreme position, the current theory goes too far. For on this view it becomes impossible to understand the joint fact of progress and adaptation — in other words, to understand how the experience of the race, which after all is only accumulated individual experience, helps to promote development and to mould it in line with the environment. Unless the “trial and error” experiments of individuals produce some racial result — if, in other words, every individual throughout the ages has to begin learning all over again from scratch — organic progress becomes unintelligible, if not impossible.

The dilemma is indeed a most formidable one for the Theory of Evolution. On the one hand, there is the negative evidence — the absence of any clear and indisputable case where mere individual modifications have been transmitted to offspring. On the other hand, there are the very many cases in which the disappearance of certain characters can only be satisfactorily explained on the assumption that modifications due to disuse of an organ have become hereditary. Again, there are still more numerous cases in which parts of the body have been constantly used in certain ways and have finally become specialised organs, with which animals are now born ready-made. There is also the class of cases mentioned by Herbert Spencer in his controversy with Weismann, and never satisfactorily answered — for instance, where the sensitivity of the finger or tongue (now hereditary) is compared with the much smaller sensitivity of the back or other parts of the body, which have never been used as organs of touch or taste. Above all, there is the difficulty — one might almost say the impossibility — of understanding organic Evolution at all, if its advance depends only on chance variations in reproduction and remains uninfluenced by the work, the experience, the trial-and-error learning, and the consequent modifications of the individuals that make up a race or species. While it is admitted, and intelligible, that purely artificial and isolated modifications — such as cutting off the tails of dogs or sheep continuously for thousands of years — will have no germinal and no hereditary effect, the case may be quite different with modifications that arise from the frequent or constant activity of the animal and that register the routine of its life. Such modifications are far more intimate to the animal organism, and may over time produce so deep an impression on the body-cells as to penetrate to the germ-cells and register a change there, which thereafter leads to hereditary and apparently spontaneous variation. Nothing in principle directly rules out such an assumption, and it does make intelligible the progressive evolution and specialisation of bodily organs that is otherwise very hard to understand. The lack of direct experimental evidence for this view is not a fatal objection. The laboratory of Nature is very different from that of experimental research. Life has not been made in the latter; it was made in the former. The slow, intimate operations extending over thousands and even millions of years — such as those that have produced most of the organic species we know — are not on a par with our recent researches in experimental evolution. Here the time factor is all-important. With all our chemical knowledge, we can never hope to rival in our laboratories the results that Nature has achieved through countless ages, say, in the crucible of the geological record. Still less can we hope to achieve through biological experiments in the laboratory what her silent processes have accomplished over millions of years.

In this connection, the recent work of Harrison and others on melanism (excessive dark pigmentation) in moths is very important. It was found that, among English native moths, species after species with pale ground colour gave rise to forms so heavily pigmented that they appeared in some cases to be dark grey and in others perfectly black. This chemical clue appears undoubtedly to be an important one and well worth pursuing more widely. (See Harrison’s note in Nature, pp. 127–9, of 22 January 1927.)

If we set aside this negative and inconclusive evidence, we find nothing in theory or fact to prevent us from regarding modifications as having an indirect influence — through more or less long biological periods — on the germ-cells. On the hypothesis of the “field,” which we have found useful before, we may regard these somatic (bodily) modifications as in the first instance influencing the field of the germ-cells, and only later, in the course of time, becoming incorporated from the field into the hereditary structure of the germ-cell.

We come, in effect, to regard modifications as in part the material out of which variations have been formed. Modifications due to constant use or disuse, or to permanent changes in the conditions of the physical environment, in the first instance influence the field of the germ-cell, and are thus the earlier phase of the later hereditary structural variations. In fact, we may say that modifications stand to variations as variations stand to specific characters. Throughout organic Nature we find this grand calculus (cumulative summing-up) at work — adding up and conserving whatever in the experience and development of the individual is of survival value to the race, and carrying on this organic summation with a fineness and delicacy surpassing that of any merely mechanical calculus. Whatever is not yet incorporated into the hereditary structure remains preserved in the invisible “field” until it is finally accentuated enough to become so incorporated. Nothing of value is lost — traces and residues (residua) of organic reactions, reflexes and tropisms (involuntary movements toward or away from a stimulus), instincts and intelligence, are all conserved or registered in the field until, in the lapse of time, they are ready to become part of the physical structure. There is no reason — except our ignorance of the facts — why modifications should not, to a large extent, be the conditions precedent (necessary preconditions) of variations. Only in this way can we explain why the trend of Variation is on the whole in harmony with the experience and the past of living Nature; why Evolution makes its forward steps along the road on which it is already moving, instead of making incalculable twists and turns, as it might do if its course were merely dependent on purely accidental, arbitrary and unmotivated variations.

That modifications of a certain intimate bodily kind, continued through many generations, may eventually influence the germ-cells and even modify their hereditary structure, is easier for us to appreciate than it was for Weismann. It is only recently that we have come to understand the important functions performed by the hormones secreted by the ductless glands (endocrine glands) in regulating and balancing our whole bodily economy. We now know that the germ-cells, far from being independent of the developed system of body-cells, have — even apart from their reproductive functions — a most intimate regulative effect in co-ordinating the functioning of the bodily system as a whole. If there is this open door between them, there is no reason why there should not also be the reverse influence of the body-cells on the germ-cells.

This question of how non-hereditary modifications are conserved brings us to another difficulty that evolutionists have found very hard to explain on the accepted Darwinian principles. I refer to the natural selection of small variations. How can small variations be selected and conserved in the struggle for existence until they are marked enough to become specific? To begin with, they are so small that it is hard to see how they can have any survival value at all. Take an organ that is being differentiated out from the rest of the body-cells. At the beginning, any variation must be utterly insignificant and practically valueless in the struggle for life, and Natural Selection has really nothing to work on. How then could an animal with such a tiny variation be selected as being more adapted to its environment? It is this awkward question that has led to the hypothesis that only very marked varieties or mutations are selected. Various more or less ingenious attempts have been made to answer this question, but to my mind they are all more or less unsatisfactory. The result is that we cannot understand how the Darwinian machinery of Natural Selection is set in motion in any particular case. Once individuals with marked specific or varietal differences exist in great numbers, we can understand why the struggle for existence between them will take place and Natural Selection will become operative. But on Darwinian principles as ordinarily understood, these marked differences between individuals can only arise from a prior selection among variations so minute that there is apparently nothing substantial enough for Natural Selection to work on. In other words, Natural Selection moves all right once it has been set in motion, but Darwinism fails to set it in motion in the first place.

In my view, the difficulty can only be satisfactorily removed by the principle of Holism, as I shall now proceed to explain. In the meantime, however, I want to point out how my suggestion — that modifications influence the field of the germ-cells and prepare the way for variations — can prove helpful to Darwinism in its predicament. On that suggestion, the small initial variation does not stand by itself, on its own merits, so to speak. It appears already powerfully supported in the struggle for existence. Individual use and practice over many generations are on its side. It does not appear as a stray, helpless infant in a hostile world; it appears in a friendly — one might say a prepared — universe. It has a stalwart nurse in the use and routine of the individual in which it appears. It is protected, sheltered and reinforced in its struggle by this constant use and routine. A small variation in the direction of a nascent (just-emerging) organ, for instance, finds itself in line with the traditional use of generations of individuals, who powerfully support it in the struggle against contrary variations. Under the shelter of this use, it develops and beats its competitors, until in the end it can fend for itself and engage in the struggle on its own account.

This explanation applies not only to variations in developing organs that are supported by use and practice on the part of a long line of individuals. It also applies to cases where permanent changes in the physical conditions impress themselves continuously on the organism. The growth-forms of plants under particular ecological conditions, for instance, are such as almost to require the view that ecological modifications — caused by the direct, silent, long-continued pressure of the environment — eventually become variations. The sameness or close resemblance of growth-forms under the same physical conditions, as seen for example in the general characters of plant formations and associations in the vegetable kingdom, is probably in some measure due to the age-long operation of ecological factors that have impressed themselves on plant development and have produced modifications which have finally become variations.

If we grant that, do we not thereby simply admit that the distinction between fluctuating variations and mutations is not absolute? It is hard to see any other rational explanation of the wide-reaching facts of homoplastic adaptation (the independent evolution of similar features in unrelated lineages), such as are shown with exceptional profusion in the ancient class of the ferns, and are evident in plants at large. (In this quotation, “fluctuating variations” correspond to what have above been called modifications, while “mutations” correspond to what have been called variations.) The experiments of Kammerer and Dürkhen on animals and plants seem to point in the same direction. But they require further corroboration, and have been seriously called into question.

The resulting general features of plant formations and associations are no doubt partly due to Natural Selection, but in part the physical environment has probably exercised a direct pressure all its own, producing an effect that has powerfully reinforced the results of Natural Selection. The hereditary variation eventually appears, but it does so not accidentally or out of the blue, but as a result of the long-continued stimulus of environmental conditions that have influenced and affected the field of the germ-cell.

While some variations thus have their roots in the traditional use and practice of individuals or in the conditions of the physical environment, and can survive under the protection thus afforded them, many variations cannot be accounted for in this way and probably arise in what appears to us as a spontaneous, independent, more or less sudden and accidental manner. The way in which they are selected and survive still has to be accounted for. Before doing so, it is worth mentioning a third set of difficulties that Darwinism faces in explaining organic Evolution. I refer to the phenomena of co-ordination and co-adaptation of organs and characters, which it is almost impossible to account for satisfactorily on orthodox Darwinian lines.

I have so far spoken of variations as if they came singly in the evolution of organisms. But they rarely appear as single units. Generally they appear in associated groups. A small variation is generally found to be accompanied by a number of still smaller associated variations. If an organ varies, the associated muscles, nerves and other body-cells undergo a corresponding variation. The evolution of the horns of a wild beast, for instance, means minor and consequential adjustments to its head, neck, muscular system, the development of the forepart of the body, and its relation to the hindquarters, as well as to many other parts and details of its body. And when we come to the question — already so difficult — of the selection of a small variation in such a horn, we are confronted with the still more hopeless difficulty of having at the same time to account for many other minor correlated variations, each of which has to be selected. Besides this, there is also their joint and associated use or functioning, which has to be accounted for as a factor in their selection. We are obviously placing a load on the principle of Natural Selection that is more than it can bear. It is being arbitrarily and artificially applied far beyond the area of its natural and proper application. And it is here that Natural Selection breaks down completely. The whole body is a system of co-ordinated structures and functions, and its origin and development can only be represented as a complex movement forward in time of a mass of associated variations that have resulted in the most marvellous co-adaptation of structures and co-ordinated functions. Faced with the problem of this complex yet orderly evolution, Natural Selection stands baffled. It can deal with individuals and with markedly formed and developed characters, but not with their delicately adjusted and associated infinitesimals (the countless extremely small accompanying changes).

The fault, however, lies not so much with Natural Selection as with our basic conceptions of the organism. Our crude, uncritical mechanistic conceptions are the real source of the difficulty, and Holism appears to me to be the way out. The root of the error lies in our disregard of the individual organism as a living whole, and in our attempt to isolate its characters from this whole and study them separately, as if they were merely mechanical components of it. The fatal mistake involved in this procedure has already been fully exposed in previous chapters. The whole is not a mechanical aggregate, indifferent to and without influence on its parts. It is itself an active factor that controls and shapes the functions of its parts. The parts bear the impress of its directive influence, and apart from this it is vain to speculate on their characters and activities. Whereas mechanical action is isolable and additive — so that the total activities of a system are represented by the sum of all the individual activities — the situation is entirely different in the case of a living whole. Here all action, as we have seen, is holistic, not only that of the whole itself but also that of the parts. The stamp of Holism is impressed on the activities of the parts no less than on the individual whole itself. The individual and its parts are reciprocally means and end to one another; neither is merely self-regarding, but each supports the other in the moving dynamic equilibrium that we call life. And so it happens that the central control of the whole also maintains and assists the parts, and the functions of the parts are always directed toward the conservation and fulfilment of the whole. With this conception of living unity and holistic action in an organism before us, let us try once more to read the riddle of Variation and Natural Selection as the twin factors in Evolution.

In the first place, we realise that each individual organism is a unitary system whose innermost nature is its own balanced self-maintenance and self-development as a whole. Heredity is just the expression of this self-conservative character. The organism, both as structure and as field — while carrying with it the past, which is its expressed self — also carries with it the still unrealised future that flows organically from that past, and it maintains a living, moving harmony between the two. Its present existing self is the more or less harmonious realisation, in its present, of the organic unity of its past and its future. Variations arise as the tentacles it throws out under environmental stimulation toward the future — a stretching of hands, dimly and unconsciously, toward future adjustment, welfare and improvement. These variations, while apparently accidental and uncontrolled, arise from the stimulus of the environment and are under the central control of the organism as a whole.

Let us for a moment consider the appearance of a small variation. It is really neither spontaneous nor accidental. It is the expression of the moving, developing organism as a whole in a particular direction. It is normally conditioned by what has gone before in the organism’s history, and is really of a piece with the organism as a whole. Nor does it, as a rule, appear alone. The organism as a whole is on the march, and while the variation may be the first and most significant indication of the inner movement, the advance is not confined to a single point but is represented by a curve of progress on which other minor advances are registered at the same time. Thus, variation A, when closely examined, will be seen to be really more like A + b + c + d, where b, c and d represent minor variations that adjust A in various respects to the organism. The apparently isolated variation is seen for what it really is — an advance of the organism as a whole in a particular direction; a holistic, as distinct from a singular and mechanical, variation or change. Mechanical analogies may help us to understand to some extent what happens. A mechanical system of a given number of elements in equilibrium is given a push or a blow with a certain force in a certain direction. When it has recovered from the push or blow and is once more in equilibrium, the change will be found to be not only in the direction in which the force was applied: all the other elements have also been affected and have undergone adjustments in order to reach the new equilibrium. The same happens — only much more intensely, intimately and organically — in the case of a change in a living whole. Variation A necessarily involves a number of collateral adjustments that depend on A and are not independently originated or conserved. In other words, holistic variation — variation of a whole in some particular respect — is the cause and carrier of minor variations that are not independently selected or conserved, and for which Natural Selection therefore need not be called into action. It is really the whole that does the “selection” in the exercise of its central control. We may call it a case of Holistic Selection, as distinct from external Natural Selection. Variation A of the whole, which is the expression of an inner urge of the whole and is therefore supported by the whole, carries with it the minor and consequential adjustments involved in variations b, c and d.

This explains one of the main difficulties we encountered above — namely, the question of the selective co-ordination of subsidiary adjustments. But the main difficulty remains: how is variation A itself selected after its appearance? How is the main small variation — perhaps too insignificant for Natural Selection to get a grip on — selected and conserved in the holistic system? If it were merely an accidental occurrence, with nothing more behind it, it would be a toss-up whether it is saved or lost, and generally it would be lost. Given the prodigality (lavish abundance) of life itself, organic changes are scattered broadcast like seeds, and most of them — with no particular urge of the organism behind them to give them continuous momentum — perish as soon as they are born. But some are in a different position. They lie in the main direction of development; they are on the road, so to speak, on which the organism is travelling; they have the whole weight of the organism behind them; they are nursed and cared for, figuratively speaking; and in the end they survive. Once again, this is a case of Holistic Selection as distinct from Natural Selection. And sometimes, as we have seen, in such cases the organism has long before the appearance of the variation begun to move in its direction. The functioning of the organism has anticipated its future structure. It has for many generations devoted a part of itself to a particular use; the part has consequently undergone modification; from an undifferentiated system of cells it has been modified in certain respects so as to anticipate an organ. When finally, in the course of time, this modification is superseded by and merged into an organic variation, that variation is in direct harmony with the needs and the practice of the organism as a whole; the practice continues alongside the variation and becomes accentuated; the pressure of the needs of the organism is behind the variation and probably increases; and the variation, covered by the habitual practice of the organism and urged forward by the organic needs, makes headway and has a fair chance of survival. It has a distinct advantage; the dice are loaded in its favour by the nature, pressure and practice of the organism as a whole. These forces behind it are probably strong enough to keep it going, though only at the very slow pace at which all biological Evolution moves. Eventually, when it has developed enough to add a perceptible measure of strength to the parent organism, it will reward its parent for its long (secular, i.e. age-long) support, will join forces with it, and will fight a victorious battle against its competitors. At this stage the belated force of Natural Selection has arrived on the scene — but not earlier, the earlier phases having depended on what I call Holistic Selection.

The Holistic Selection that acts within each organism among its parts inter se (among themselves) is essentially different from the Natural Selection that operates between different organisms — which is more appropriately called the struggle for existence. Holistic Selection is much more subtle in its operation, and much more social and friendly in its activity. It puts the inner resources of the organism behind the promising variation, however weak and feeble it may be in comparison with other characters, and lets it win through powerful backing rather than through the ruthless scrapping of less desirable variations. Within the organism, the battle is not always to the strong, nor is the struggle an unregulated scrimmage in which the most virile survive. The whole is all the time on the scene as an active, friendly arbiter and regulator, and its favours go to those variations that lie along the road of its own development, efficiency and perfection.

The continuous Holistic Selection of small variations may be compared to the survival of obsolete organs in an organism. Both are carried forward by the organism as a whole — perhaps for millions of years — without being directly useful to the organism in either case. The whole, to speak metaphorically, takes long views, both into the future and into the past, and mere considerations of present utility do not weigh very heavily with it. It carries its infant variation along in the same way that it carries its aged and dying parts or atrophying organs. Both are borne along, covered and shielded by the main characters of the organism. From the point of view of survival value, as from so many other points of view, the whole is more important than any of its parts. And so it comes about that the organism is a most complicated system — a present living unity embodying its far-away past no less than its dim distant future. The whole controls, guides and conserves all. The fate of any particular part, considered by itself and on its own merits, would be an inexplicable mystery, and might be expected to be the very opposite of what actually happens in practice. When, however, it is viewed from its position and function in the whole, the mystery is explained; we see how different the laws of life are from the laws of mechanics, and how wrong it is to apply mechanistic and atomistic conceptions in a region where Holism prevails.

To understand how a small variation comes to be favoured, we may picture an organism as a moving, developing equilibrium, which is never perfectly adjusted because it has a persistent slight overbalance in the direction of development. Complete equilibrium is never reached, and would be fatal if it were, since it would mean stagnation, atrophy and death. And so the overbalance in a certain direction, or with a definite orientation, continues indefinitely, and all small developments and adjustments and “variations” that have that specific orientation have the momentum of the whole behind them, and tend to survive and grow, while those in other directions are dropped and discarded. We may accordingly say that, in each case, “the whole” is a co-worker with its small variations that will eventually prove useful — that as an active factor its influence is on the side of such small variations, and that with this inner nurture and support these small variations are, in their infant stages, practically independent of external support for their survival and steady evolution.

The activity of the whole is seen not only in the maintenance and evolution of the small variation and all the subordinate adjustments that go with it, but also and especially in all the countless co-ordinations and co-adaptations of structure and function that make up a living organism. I believe it is generally admitted that this phenomenon of organic co-ordination cannot be satisfactorily explained on mechanical principles. The functioning of an animal as a whole has something unique about it, and the term “whole” in this connection is no mere phrase but a fact of vital significance. We have already considered the matter fully in Chapter VI. Here we shall only add that to suppose that Natural Selection has not only brought about the separate organs of animals and their functions, but also satisfactorily accounts for their adjustments to one another and their co-ordinated activities in animal behaviour, is to suppose what certainly has never been and cannot be explained in detail, and what is probably in conflict with the facts of development. Intelligent and purposive action by a human or other animal cannot be explained on mechanical principles; nor can instinctive action, nor even the reflex or organic activities and functions below the level of instinct or intelligence. An animal — even of the lowest type — makes an unconscious effort to catch food or beat off an enemy, and in the process performs a large number of acts that are all effectively co-ordinated toward the attainment of its object. No mechanical explanation of this process of co-ordinated movements has ever been given. The animal acts as a whole, with a unity and effectiveness of action that is not just the mechanical sum of its movements. The concept of the whole is the only category that will explain such unity. Co-ordination and co-adaptation in organic structure and behaviour cannot be explained on any other ground.

So far we have considered Holism as inclusive of variations — as a process regulating and co-ordinating groups of actualised variations and organic characters generally. But this by no means exhausts the scope of Holism in organic development. It includes not only the production of variations but, just as much, the repression of variations. Holism is as often inhibitive as creative; certain characters are held back at the same time as others are pushed forward, and in this way a balanced movement and progress of the organic whole is secured. When, for instance, the form and characteristics of a gorilla are compared with the human type, it becomes clear that in human evolution certain tendencies have been definitely held in check, and the utter caricature in appearance that would have resulted from unrestrained development has thus been prevented. Nobody who ignores this negative aspect of Evolution can possibly understand the present forms of animals as compared with their living or fossil affiliations. Tendencies to variation that were realised in the case of Neanderthal man have been more or less severely repressed in the present human races. If there had been unrestrained evolution of all potential variations, the results would have been truly dreadful in their grotesqueness. In fact, we find at work in organic Evolution an influence not unlike what at a much later stage we recognise as the ethical control of feelings, impulses and instinctive movements of an undesirable character. The whole in personality — the whole in its ethical flowering in the human — means not only the expression of certain moral qualities, but also and equally the repression of others. Elements and tendencies that we find strongly operative in our instinctive or organic nature we have to keep in check, hold down severely, and prevent from emerging in our characters as a whole. This is the very essence of Holism in its mature ethical development. There is something very similar, and equally fundamental, in the activity of Holism in the earlier purely organic phases of Evolution. In any individual organism, the whole is in control, pushing forward some tendencies and holding others back, expressing some variations and repressing others — and through all of this maintaining a mobile equilibrium of all the elements, positive and negative, that are uniquely blended in the individual. So, if we wish to understand the details of organic Evolution in any particular case, we should look for the repressions no less than for the variations; it is the combination of the two that constitutes Evolution.

I shall no doubt be asked what experimental verification there is for the holistic view of Evolution set forth here. My answer is to repeat what I have already said: that natural Evolution, as distinguished from experimental Evolution, is a process not of the hour or the day but of geological time, and that the results — matured and consolidated through immemorial periods — cannot be repeated or rehearsed by short-term laboratory experiments, conducted under conditions very different from those of Nature. These experiments, however valuable and instructive in offering subsidiary clues and hints about the natural process, by no means exhaust or even seriously affect the real problem of creative Evolution; and a correct view of Evolution must take account of this difference and be based on an intelligent appreciation of the natural processes rather than on the very limited data yielded by our laboratory experiments. There is no doubt that experimental Evolution has, through its unavoidable limitations, greatly blurred the great Darwinian vision of organic Evolution, and instead of making us realise more fully its truth, effectiveness and grandeur as a whole, has tended to deflect our attention to particular problems that are special and limited enough to be capable of laboratory treatment. The special and exceptional cases of Mutation and Hybridisation come to be regarded as covering the entire process of organic Evolution. My aim in this chapter has been, through a re-examination of the position thus created, to explore and reconnoitre a way back to the broader and wider view of Evolution. And in doing so, I have sought the help of a concept that we have found at work not only in organic Evolution, but in all organic structures and processes, and even, to a limited extent, in inorganic Nature itself. I shall now briefly summarise the results we have reached in this chapter, in order to see how they bear on the wide Darwinian conception from which we started.

The relative importance of the internal and external factors in Evolution has materially altered since Darwin’s time. Variation has become much more important than Natural Selection — not only in biological studies and experimental research, but also in our view of it as an operative factor in organic Evolution. While remaining a substantial and important factor, Natural Selection has yielded pride of place to Variation. The factor of intense struggle and competition in Nature, on which Darwin — following the Malthusian clue (the idea, drawn from Malthus, that populations grow faster than their resources) — laid so much stress, is now seen not only to be relatively less important but also to bear a somewhat different character from what it had on Darwin’s view. The struggle for existence is, like Mutation, an exceptional and not the usual procedure of organic Nature. This world is at bottom a friendly universe, in which organised tolerant co-existence is the rule and destructive warfare the exception, resorted to only when the balance of Nature is seriously disturbed. Normally, Natural Selection takes the form of comradeship, of social co-operation and mutual help. Normally, too, the organic struggle is very much in abeyance (suspended, held in check), and the silent, effortless, constant pressure of the physical and organic environment exercises a very powerful influence. The young science of Ecology has been built up since Darwin’s time, and is based on the recognition of this fact: that, in addition to the operation of Natural Selection, the environment has a silent, assimilative, transformative influence of a very profound and enduring character on all organic life. In the subtle ways of Nature, sun and earth, night and day, and all the things of earth and air and sea mingle silently with life, sink into it, and become part of its structure. And in response to this profound stimulus, life grows and evolves — the lesser whole in harmony with the greater whole of Nature.

The interaction between the inner and the outer factors in Evolution is far closer and subtler than one would gather from Darwinism, either in its earlier or in its later Genetic form. It is not merely a matter of one factor creating variations and another eliminating some of these creations and leaving the rest free, which are then said to be selected for perpetuation. The inner creative factor in some measure acts directly under the stimulus of the external factor, and the variations that emerge are the result of this intimate interaction. The isolation of the inner from the outer factor, so much emphasised by the Mendelians, is — despite its apparent agreement with observation — really a mistaken assumption, based on neglect of the time factor in Evolution. The environment is a great stimulus of variation, and even more so is the somatic (bodily) organism itself, which is closer to the germ-cell than the environment is.

We can only understand the process of organic Evolution if we assume that, deeply as the germ-cell carriers of Variation are hidden from external contacts, they are not completely or for ever isolated from them — that changes due to habitual behaviour or to environmental, chemical or ecological pressure affect the “field” of the germ-cells, and, if sufficiently long-continued and intense, sooner or later penetrate the structures of these germ-cells, and stimulate and set in motion the internal factor of Variation. The response comes back in a crisis of variation or mutation that permanently alters the internal hereditary structure. In these cases, the inner and outer factors of Evolution do not operate independently and by opposed and contrasted methods; they collaborate in the closest manner, as the stimulus and response that we find distinctive of all organic action. From this external factor, which operates as a stimulus of organic variation, we have to distinguish Darwin’s Natural Selection — which is another external factor, operative not in connection with the stimulation of variations but in connection with their subsequent elimination or destruction, and acting like a sieve through which all life has to pass on pain of destruction. The external factors in Evolution are therefore, on this view, twofold: the environmental or ecological factor, which to some extent influences or induces variation; and the factor of organic struggle, which sets in motion the warfare among organisms for the limited goods of life — what Darwin called Natural Selection.

But it is only in certain classes of cases that the “use” factor or the external or ecological stimulus of variation comes into play. In others, the stimulus of variation is entirely internal, and must be sought in the fresh mixture and re-arrangement of the chromatin elements (the chromosome material) of the germ-cell nucleus at certain critical stages in the evolutionary process — such as in the sexual reproduction of some organisms, or in the endomixis (a process of internal nuclear reorganisation) and rejuvenescence (renewal of vitality) that occur at certain stages in others.

This internal factor in Variation and Evolution has supplied a suggestive clue for research in Genetics. But to view this factor as purely mechanical must lead to great difficulties in detail, and make it impossible to understand the process of organic Evolution as a whole. I have therefore tried to stress the contrary view of this inner factor — to show that it is holistic in character and operation, that it thereby resolves the difficulties the mechanistic hypothesis has created for itself, and that it leads to a reconciliation of the two factors operative in Evolution. The theory of Holism has thus, once again, though in a way different from that envisioned by Darwin, brought us back to the great Darwinian vision of universal adaptation.

Footnote: I am afraid that the current “gene” theory, especially as it is being worked out and applied by Professor T. H. Morgan and others in their important researches, is far too deeply tainted with mechanistic elements. They look for organic change in individual genes rather than in the intra-organic field or milieu (the inner organic environment) of interacting and mutually modifying functionings. There may soon be more of the hypothetical “genes” than the nucleus or the chromosomes can bear. Here too, the concept of the whole — as the centre and source of modifications in a network of connected influences and functions — may appear as the way out of the difficulty. The organic or holistic concept should be applied in all its subtle implications, and should not be translated into a sort of chemistry of genes.

But it has done more: it has enabled us to realise the pervasive creative unity that makes all the diverse elements of existence the co-operative members and inhabitants of an essentially friendly universe. Operating as the inner creative factor at the heart of things, it has led to the evolution of a universe in which all the factors and products — organic and inorganic alike — are not alien to and destructive of each other, but are capable of mutual adaptation and adjustment, just because they share a common origin and have an indisputable, though often scarcely recognisable, family relationship. This is not to assume a Pre-established Harmony (Leibniz’s idea that all parts of the universe were arranged in advance to act in concert), which would be as great a mistake in one direction as the contrary and more usual mechanistic assumption — that universal adaptation and organic co-ordination are in effect the accidental results of utterly unconnected factors — would be in the other. The true conception, not only for philosophy but also for science, is that of parts, elements or aspects within a whole. It is the high task of science to explore the mechanisms of adaptation and variation in all their details, and to pursue at all costs the chemistry and physics of the cell, of which we still know so little. But it must do so in the light of the unifying, regulating, co-ordinating activity of the holistic factor, which — even from a purely scientific point of view — is just as important as the study of the special mechanisms. Above all, biological science must always keep before itself the standpoint of the whole, without which all the details, far from being recognised as organic to one another, are mere loose, meaningless items, like the sands of the seashore — utterly useless for understanding the unique unity that constitutes an organic individual. The whole is the ultimate category of organic explanation, just as it is the ultimate feature of organic adaptation and evolution. More than this: Holism is also creative of all values. Take the case of organic Beauty. It is undeniable that Beauty rests on a holistic basis. Beauty is essentially a product of Holism and is inexplicable apart from it. Beauty is of the whole; Beauty is a relation of parts within a whole — a blending of elements of form and colour, of foreground and background, of expression and suggestion, of structure and function, of structure and field — which is perceived and appreciated as harmonious and satisfying, according to laws that it is for Aesthetics (Esthetics, the branch of philosophy concerned with beauty) to determine.

It may be questioned how far the phenomena of repression alongside expression in organic Evolution, of regulated development as a whole, of beauty, and of similar phenomena can properly be brought under the Darwinian factors. Perhaps it is better to recognise that there is something wider and deeper at work in Evolution than the factors identified by Darwin and his successors — something of which those factors are themselves only an expression. The whole is itself an active feature or factor, and its activity as such explains phenomena that it is difficult, if not impossible, to account for in any other way without very forced interpretations. The inner sources of wealth and beauty in Nature are inexhaustible, and they are poured forth with a lavish hand in the creative process of Evolution. It is not merely survival values on Darwinian lines that count: on the foundation of variations with survival value is raised a superstructure of development that far transcends that narrow basis. Mind, in its marvellous human flowering (efflorescence), no doubt rests on a basis of survival value — but how much more it is than that! The glories of art and literature, the peace of mystic religious experience, the creative Ideals that lift this life beyond the limitations of its lowly origin — all these experiences and developments have built a new spiritual world on the humble foundations of survival values. In the kingdom of life is visibly arising its capital, the City of God. Apart from the great human development, beauty in Nature tells the same story. The song of birds, with its primary appeal to sex but with so infinitely much more in it than mere sex-appeal; the glorious forms and colouring of birds, beasts and insects, which no doubt arise in and from the struggle for existence but finally rise above it and strip it of all its sordidness and drabness; above all, the wonder of plants and flowers, which were meant for the eyes of birds and insects, but contain so infinitely much more than the eye of bird or insect ever beheld or ever can behold — it is everywhere in Nature the same. Everywhere we see the great overplus (overflow, surplus) of the whole. So little is asked; so much more is given. The female only asks for a sign by which to recognise the male, and to help her select and enjoy him in preference to others. And in answer she gets an overpowering revelation of beauty, out of all proportion to her modest request. The peahen has no discriminating understanding of the wondrous colouring of the peacock, which far transcends even our human powers; but in some inscrutable way something of an emotional nature in her takes it all in, and is satisfied. It is deep calling unto deep; it is the whole appealing to the whole. There is evidently more in all this than the Darwinian factors can satisfactorily explain, and it would be both foolish and unscientific not to recognise this frankly. To me the conclusion of the matter is this: the inexhaustible whole is itself at work; Holism is an active factor influencing and interacting with the particular Darwinian factors; not only its trend but also its output far exceeds the immediate present utilities and needs of organic Evolution; and its bow is bent for distant horizons, far beyond all human power of vision and understanding.
