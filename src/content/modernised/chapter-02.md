Summary: It is not only in organic Evolution that the old fixed concepts and units of thought are breaking down. Recent advances in physical science have extended the revolution to the inorganic world too. The fixity of the atom has now followed the fixity of biological species into the limbo of obsolete ideas. In many directions we now need new concepts — ones that are more in keeping with the fluid, creative process of nature.

We start with the new ideas of Space and Time, which in the system of Relativity are taking the place of the old Newtonian concepts that are still commonly accepted. The new ideas of Space and Time arose from research in advanced mathematics and physics, and they were originally concerned with the fact that all actual motion in the universe is relative, and with the mathematical and physical consequences of that relativity. The mathematical physicists assumed that, to a moving observer, a body moving at a velocity close to that of light appears to contract — to be shorter than it would seem to a stationary observer — and that the faster either of them moves, the greater the contraction becomes. Time varies in a similar way, but in the opposite direction: while the space of the moving body appears to contract, its time appears to expand, so that it takes longer to pass a given point than it would if seen by a stationary observer. This joint and inseparable variation of Space and Time was not only enormously important in itself; it led directly to the revolutionary idea that neither Space nor Time exists independently, but that together they form the Space-Time medium of the real physical world. From this point of view, bodies and things considered as merely spatial are not real but abstractions, while events — which involve both Space and Time — are real and form the actual units of reality. The dethronement of the old Space and Time and their replacement by Space-Time has been tested in the most rigorous ways, both in the immense world of astronomy and in the most minute world of the atom, and in both cases the new concepts have been found to work satisfactorily.

THE variation of Space and Time has led to a further conclusion. In a world of relative motion such as ours — where observers and observed bodies are moving at velocities like that of light — all standards of measurement and all clocks of Time are themselves variable and yield no constant results. But a varying standard must produce a warped or twisted field. Applying this conclusion to gravitation and to the rotational motions of the universe, Einstein found that the Space-Time medium of the universe is curved and warped, and not the homogeneous (uniform throughout) medium that the old ideas attributed to Space and Time. In the neighbourhood of matter — that is, in all gravitational fields — events happen along curves and follow the curves of Space-Time. The result is that the entire universe acquires a definite structural character, and is not a diffuse uniformity as was formerly supposed. According to the new Space-Time concept, structure — definite, organised structure — becomes the essential characteristic of the physical universe and its vast fields, and this structural character explains many phenomena that were previously inexplicable. Newton’s and Kant’s ideas of Space and Time are then compared with those of Einstein.

In the previous chapter I tried to explain how accepting the theory of Evolution must inevitably and profoundly affect our view of the nature of matter. In this chapter I go on to ask what bearing recent far-reaching physical research and theory have on this position. Our task is to break free from the hard, narrow conceptions of the Victorian age, to see Nature once more in her fluid and creative plasticity, and to reformulate our concepts from this deeper point of view. A great change has come over our view of Nature — a change large enough, in the end, to amount to one of the fundamental revolutions in human thought. But we are still in the middle of that change, and so it is hard for us to grasp its full significance. Three dates stand out in bold relief as marking the beginning of this change: 1859, when Darwin’s Origin of Species was published; 1896, when Becquerel discovered Radioactivity; and 1915, when Einstein published his General Theory of Relativity. Around these three great events, other discoveries of profound interest have taken place and continue to take place. As a result, our entire viewpoints and standpoints with regard to Nature and reality are undergoing a fundamental change — one that must in the end affect every province of human thought and conduct. The fixity of organic species is gone; the fixity of inorganic elements is gone; the evenness of space and time is gone. The picture is once again becoming fluid; the old rigid order is visibly dissolving; the fixed landmarks and beacon-points by which earlier generations steered their course in science are sinking from view. The task awaits the future of building, out of this fluid situation and these instabilities, a new stable world of ideas — one in closer harmony with the reality around us and within us. One aspect of Evolution has already briefly engaged our attention in the last chapter, and other aspects of it will be considered in Chapter VIII. In the present chapter we must turn to Einstein’s General Theory of Relativity and the bearing it has on our ideas of space and time as the framework in which events are located, and the medium in which Evolution takes place. The resulting view of the universe as structural — and of structure or configuration as fundamental to the universe and all its forms — is important for the subject and the argument of this work.

People get frightened when they are invited to consider Einstein’s theory. Its refined abstractions, its difficult mathematical form, its complete novelty and its reversal of ordinary common-sense viewpoints make it a terror to the uninitiated. And yet I believe the Einstein viewpoint can be put quite simply and intelligibly. Indeed, it must be put that way if it is ever to become part and parcel of ordinary educated thought. We must distinguish between the simple, clear viewpoint itself and the abstruse mathematical processes by which it was reached, and the technical mathematical form in which it is expressed. For ordinary purposes, the viewpoint can be separated from those things. Understanding and appreciating the Relativity viewpoint does not depend on knowing the process by which Einstein arrived at it. The result is quite distinct from the process. It is like groping our way through a long, dark, rough tunnel and finally emerging into the clear daylight at the far end: it is not necessary, in order to appreciate the new view, to plunge back into the dark tunnel. Besides, I must frankly state my own layman’s impression that the Einstein theory — as distinct from the broad viewpoint that has been reached — has not yet found its final expression. All great truths are essentially simple, and the absence of simplicity in the present statement only shows that the ultimate form has not yet been reached. The day may yet come when the ten obscure Einstein equations of gravitation appear as merely the scaffolding of a simpler structure yet to arise — one whose naturalness and inevitability will be as obvious to every educated person as the heliocentric (sun-centred) conception of Copernicus has become.

Einstein’s theory originally arose from research in advanced mathematics and physics, and a brief reference to its mathematical origin will be helpful. Galileo and Newton were the fathers of modern classical mechanics; they (and especially Newton) formulated the laws of moving bodies as an exact mathematical science. Now the seed of the new Relativity mechanics is the almost obvious fact that the motion of a body is never absolute, but is always relative to some other body or point. If this body or point of reference is stationary, Newton’s laws of motion apply completely, and Euclid’s geometry (the standard ‘flat-space’ geometry of straight lines and right angles) also applies, so that the movements of bodies can be represented by geometrical figures. Such bodies are said to move in Euclidean space, which is the same and homogeneous (uniform) all the way through and in every direction. Since Newton’s time, a great deal of attention has been given to the case where the body of reference, or the observer, is itself in motion. This case is important, because it is in fact the situation of every body in our universe: every observer or point of reference is itself in motion. A point on the earth, for example, rotates with a certain velocity around the centre of the earth, while the earth in turn rotates with another velocity around the sun. The sun itself is not stationary either, but moves in relation to a star in the constellation Hercules, which is itself in motion in relation to the star stream of the Milky Way. It is this case — the moving observer or point of reference — that Einstein’s theory deals with, and so it is clear that the theory faces the problem of motion as it actually exists in the universe. Our impression as observers of being at rest or stationary is a mere illusion, and Einstein’s great service was to explore that illusion and to show in exact mathematical form how far it affects our perception and judgment of motion in the world. So let us take the case of moving observers. The original research dealt with problems of electricity and light, and it is with objects or observers moving at a velocity close to that of light that the real difficulty arises. But for simplicity, I shall replace the ray of light with an ordinary moving object. When a moving object (say a train in motion) is viewed by a moving observer (say someone in a motor-car driving along a road parallel to the train), Fitzgerald and Lorentz found it necessary to make the following assumptions to account for the facts:

(A) The train appears slightly shorter than it would to a stationary observer.

(B) The time it takes the train to pass a given point appears slightly longer (or, equivalently, the train appears to move somewhat more slowly) than it would to a stationary observer.

In other words: to a moving observer, the length or space occupied by a moving body is smaller in the direction of its motion than it would appear to a stationary observer; and likewise the time taken by the observed body to pass a point will be longer. The faster the observer or the observed body moves, the more the space and time of the observed body will vary for him, compared with what they would be if he were at rest. These two variations of space and time are joint variations: they happen simultaneously but in opposite directions — the one decreasing in proportion as the other increases for the moving observer. Space contracts and time expands in inverse proportion to one another, depending on the rate of motion of a moving body of reference, or a moving observer, when either is moving at a velocity approaching that of light. We can generalise this result and say that, so long as several observers move at different rates but each one uniformly and in straight lines relative to the others, the velocity of the moving body they are observing follows the same law for all of them, because the proportional co-variations of their respective spaces and times cancel each other out, so to speak. This is a popular way of stating the main principle of Einstein’s Special Theory of Relativity, first published in 1905 in rigorous mathematical form. It explained the fact, repeatedly confirmed by the most accurate experiments of Michelson and Morley, that the velocity of light is always the same, no matter what the velocity of its source, and however greatly the velocities of different moving observers trying to measure it may differ. An observer moving away from a flash of light at half the speed of light will see the flash at the same time as a stationary observer, not later as one might suppose. The reason is that the time and space measures of the moving observer have changed jointly so as to neutralise the effect his motion would otherwise have on his observation. For all bodies or observers moving at ordinary terrestrial speeds, the change is so small as to be negligible. It is only when one or both of them move at a rate approaching that of light that the apparent contraction and expansion become important and have to be taken into account in actual calculations.

The two key facts to keep in mind from the above are these: (1) for moving observers, clocks and standards of measurement in very fast motion are no longer absolute but vary according to the rates of motion of the observers, the clocks, or the standards themselves; and (2) there is this curious joint and opposite variation of the space and time measures of moving observers or bodies. In fact, taken separately, Space and Time must be mere abstractions, since in all actual movements they are always found inseparably acting together.

From this co-variation of Space and Time it is only a step to Minkowski’s great idea, first formulated in 1908: that in natural events Space and Time are not independent factors, and that the right way to represent events mathematically is to introduce time as a fourth dimension — not of space, but of the Space-Time continuum (the unified four-dimensional fabric in which events take place) in which events really occur. Time is, of course, in many ways unlike space and is not just another dimension of it; but the inseparable co-variation of space and time in everything that happens in nature makes it both feasible and proper to substitute the real Space-Time continuum of events for the old abstract three-dimensional space of bodies or points. In passing, it may be pointed out here that the old idea of space and time as separately real involved both the errors of analysis and abstraction that I drew attention to in the last chapter. Minkowski’s brilliant idea has simply brought us back to the natural fact as we encounter it in experience, where nothing ever happens in space alone or in time alone but always in both together, and where objects are not observed by themselves but always as elements or items in the stream of perceived events. What is more, it can easily be shown that the very ideas of Space and Time interpenetrate and depend on one another. Succession (the time-series) and co-existence (the space-series) are necessary to each other and would not even be intelligible in isolation. For succession (time) would perish at every step and would not even form a series, unless it had enduringness or co-existence (space). And co-existence (space) would stop at its first step and would not be spread out or extended unless it also had succession (time).

To Einstein, this concept of a Space-Time continuum proved most welcome and fruitful, and he went on to apply it to the explanation of all motions in the universe — not only to uniform, straight-line motions that take place in uniform Euclidean space, but also to rotating and accelerated motions that take place in a gravitational field with a non-Euclidean (curved-space) character.

His first step was to show, on purely theoretical grounds, that a body under the influence of a constant force — and therefore moving with a constantly increasing acceleration — would, to an observer on board, behave in exactly the same way as a body acted on by gravitation. So suppose a man is shut in a cage in such a way that he can’t observe any other body and can’t notice his own motion. Suppose this cage is suspended in distant space where there is no gravitation. And suppose that the cage is being pulled upward with a constant force, so that it moves faster and faster with steadily increasing acceleration. In reality, then, the case is one of acceleration. But the enclosed observer will not notice this motion and acceleration of the cage and himself. He will only feel as though he is being pulled downward by his own weight. If he drops an object from his hand, it will fall to the bottom of the cage like a stone dropped on the earth. What is more, the rate at which the object falls is the same, whatever its shape, size or amount of material. That italicised fact is distinctive of gravitation. In other words, the man in the cage will think that he, the cage, and the object inside it are all being acted on by gravitation. What is really due to acceleration appears to be a case purely and simply of gravitation. So we see that acceleration and gravitation are really the same phenomenon, differing only in appearance to observers. Acceleration and gravitation are, in fact, equivalent expressions. Einstein’s closed cage may yet become as historic as Newton’s falling apple.

Now take rotation, which is simply a special case of acceleration. Imagine an observer placed on a flat, rotating circular disc, who sets out to measure the area of the disc and the rate at which it is revolving. He has two identical clocks. He places one near the centre of the disc and the other near the circumference, in order to make some time measurements. When he checks the clock near the centre, he finds that it runs faster than the clock placed near the circumference. We have already seen why. The motion of the disc at the centre is zero, while its motion at the circumference is quite considerable, so the readings of identical clocks at these two points will differ for the observer. Likewise, the rate of any identical clock will vary according to its distance from the centre, since the motions of all points on the disc differ according to their distance from the centre, where the motion is slower. He then applies identical measuring rods and finds the same continual variation. He finds that identical measuring rods vary in length according to where they are placed on the disc: a rod placed on the circumference is shorter than one placed near the centre. And the differing lengths of the rods will measure out different spaces. The observer will become utterly confused, and will eventually conclude that the spaces on the disc are not the same everywhere and in all directions, but appear to vary in every direction, and to be twisted, warped and curved. Or, as we would say, the space of the disc is not the straight-line, homogeneous, uniform Euclidean space; it is curved and non-Euclidean. Taking the variations of space and of time on the rotating disc together, we conclude that the disc is not a Euclidean space but a non-Euclidean Space-Time continuum.

Since we have seen that the phenomena of acceleration (including rotation) and gravitation are equivalent, the considerations that applied to the rotating disc apply also to every gravitating body. We know that gravitation acts at a distance from the centre of the gravitating body — in fact every such body is surrounded by a gravitational field far larger than itself. So the non-Euclidean character will also distinguish the Space-Time continuum within that field. In other words, motions and happenings in this field will not follow the law of a uniform time and a homogeneous, identical space in all directions. They will take place along curves, just as they do on our rotating disc. A body falling through space and into such a field will, on entering it and while inside it, follow not a straight path but the curve that most closely matches its original straight path; a ray of light passing through the field will likewise follow the nearest curve rather than a straight line. And indeed any physical event within that field will, insofar as it is a translational event (one that involves a change of position from one place to another), follow the curve along which it happens to take place. These deductions from theory have been experimentally verified in the most important particulars.

On this theory, the mysterious “attractive” power of matter that we call gravitation takes on a quite different character. The apparent attraction is simply the curved or bent paths produced by the motions in the universe of masses charged with energy — motions that we ourselves do not particularly notice (except as pushes and pulls on our bodies), since we happen to share in the same motions as the phenomena we observe. This does not, however, make gravitation an unreality, due to the subjective vagaries of the observer. Gravitation, as we have seen, now becomes the curves of the real Space-Time world in the neighbourhood of matter; it marks the inevitable paths that all events must follow in the physical universe. Far from being subjective or merely relative to the observer, gravitation becomes the very structure of the real world, and reflects the stratification (layered organisation) that characterises the vast fields of the Space-Time continuum. Our whole conception of the universe is changed. Instead of imagining the universe as material bodies floating in a medium of uniform homogeneous space, we now look on masses of “matter” (themselves due to high-speed energies) as developing huge “fields,” in which the curves of the lines can be calculated and the course of events happening within them can be predicted. Events follow the curves, and their future course can be calculated once their position on the curves is determined. The physics of Nature thus becomes, in part, an annex of the geometry of Space-Time, and a new power is placed in the hands of human beings, limited only by the limits of mathematical insight and genius. The concept of the “field” becomes all-important in science and in thought. The “field” of matter is simply the curved structure of real Space-Time, which extends far beyond visible matter itself. Throughout its vast “fields,” the universe takes on a form not unlike the curved contours and unevennesses we associate with the physical appearance of this globe. The contours of the unseen Space-Time field that surrounds us follow very much the same lines that meet our eye on sea and land. But these lines are not mere empty form. They are not just curves of beauty; they are real and causal, because they determine the course of events in the universe. The peripheries of rotating bodies are such curves; the planets move around the suns along such curves; light is propagated (transmitted through space) along such curves. In fact, these curves are the pathways of the physical universe that all physical events must follow. The innermost nature of the universe is active Energy or Action, and involves the interplay of tremendous activities whose result is expressed in matter and in these curves of its field; and the curves are nothing but the actual orientation or direction of events in the Space-Time field of the material universe. Where bodies seem to be moving under the influence or compulsion of forces, they are really only following the curves of the gravitational field, and the forces are purely imaginary. There are no forces in the real world; events simply move freely along lines of curvature, which are the lines of least resistance.

What is, or would be, the situation beyond the material universe and its vast fields? There we pass beyond the bounds of gravitation, where there is neither rotation nor acceleration, and where “bodies” (if such astral abstractions could even be imagined) persist in their state of rest or of uniform motion in a straight line, in line with Newton’s First Law. There Space-Time, if it could be imagined to exist at all, would not be warped or curved, but would be homogeneous and continuous, and would be exactly the form of empty nothingness. In fact, homogeneous Euclidean Space-Time beyond all real fields is simply a limiting conception of thought, and would correspond to nothing we have any knowledge of in our universe, where the great masses of matter everywhere produce curved fields.

It may be interesting, in conclusion, to point out the difference between this conception of Space-Time in the Theory of Relativity and the conceptions of Space and Time formulated by Newton and Kant. For Newton, both Space and Time were absolutes — that is, they were real, invariable, permanent entities or characters of things and events. Each was homogeneous and continuous, and so could be adequately expressed by Euclid’s geometry. There was nothing subjective about them. For Kant, who in other respects deeply admired Newton’s system, the great problem of knowledge was how to determine the relative contributions made to our knowledge of the world by the subjective and the objective factors respectively, and especially how much (and what) the mind contributed to the common pool of knowledge and experience. His answer, in effect, was that the mind’s action is creative in experience and that it contributes to our knowledge: (a) the elements of Space and Time, which are nothing but the mind’s own sensuous forms of intuition (the basic structures the mind imposes on raw sense-data so that it can perceive anything in the first place) imposed on the materials of sense and experience; (b) the general conceptual system of knowledge that follows from the categories of the Understanding (Kant’s set of basic concepts — such as substance, cause, and unity — which the mind brings to its experience of the world); and (c) certain ultimate regulative principles of human Reason (highest-level principles, like the unity of nature, that guide our thinking but are not themselves derived from experience). On this view, then, Space and Time are nothing but the necessary forms of human sensuous perception; they do not exist in external reality, but are imposed by the mind on all objects of sense. While accepting the homogeneous, universal, Euclidean characters that Newton ascribed to Space and Time, Kant denied that they were real entities or characters of things or events. If those characters belonged to things, Kant could not see how the a priori synthetic character of mathematical knowledge would be possible (Kant’s claim that mathematical truths are both substantive — they really tell us something about the world — and yet known prior to any particular experience). He could only explain that fact by treating the sensuous form of things as a subjective contribution of the mind itself. According to him, the universal forms of Space and Time in experience were due not to the things or the world to which they seemed to belong, but purely and simply to the perceiving mind which invests all things or events with them.

In contrast to both these theories, in the Theory of Relativity Space and Time as joint co-ordinate forms belong both to the mind and to things. Einstein’s whole effort was to separate the subjective appearance from the objective reality — to separate the relative, variable and disturbing contribution made by the observing mind from the real, permanent Space-Time factor that is inherent in the physical universe. If the confirmation of theory by facts means anything, it must be admitted that Einstein has been remarkably successful in his analysis and assessment of these two aspects — subjective and objective — of the Space-Time concept. The fact that Space and Time are not, on the one hand, merely subjective conditions of experience as Kant held, nor, on the other, merely objective elements given to experience as Newton held, but that they are both subjective and objective contributions to experience, might have been the discovery of a sound psychology or epistemology (the branch of philosophy concerned with the nature, sources and limits of knowledge). But that these two factors of Space and Time have been fused into a single synthesis, in which both subjective and objective elements have been properly sorted out, isolated, evaluated and rigorously determined, is an achievement of the most outstanding importance not only for science but also for philosophy. It hardly needs pointing out that, in the Theory of Relativity, Space and Time are not metaphysical conceptions or forms. The infinite, homogeneous Space and Time that for Kant were mental forms imposed on all sense-experience, and that for Leibniz were the pre-established permanent universal order of co-existence and succession among things, are for Einstein mere limiting pseudo-concepts, metaphysical abstractions with no relation to our actual experience. In our experience, Space and Time are given elements just like colour, weight and the rest. The task of science is to co-ordinate these elements in an intelligible form, and in doing so Einstein has simply explored them as if they were genuine physical experience like the rest. The result is that certain historic errors have been eliminated from the concepts of Space and Time, and their physical qualities have been determined in line with the rest of our physical experience and concepts. The Space-Time continuum, instead of being a vague, homogeneous, formless, metaphysical concept, becomes part of physical reality — the “field” of the material world, with a definite structure of its own. Structure — real, differentiated structure — becomes the inmost form not only of matter but also of its real Space-Time field. The close bearing of this on the main argument of this work will become clear in the chapters that follow.
