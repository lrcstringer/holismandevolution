Summary: Coming now from the Space-Time continuum to Matter, we find that the feature of structure stands out far more conspicuously and importantly. The physical and chemical make-up of matter is almost entirely a matter of structure. Chemistry has traced matter back to its ultimate units — atoms — and to the way these combine into molecules and substances according to structural schemes that depend on how the different units are placed and spaced in various chemical combinations. The New Physics has carried the analysis a step further back, by breaking atoms down into their constituent electrons and protons — the units of negative and positive electricity. In our picture of fundamental matter, these units are arranged structurally so as to resemble more or less complicated solar systems, with central positive nuclei and electrons revolving around them like planets. The explanation of the physical and chemical properties of matter has been traced to the structural arrangements of these atomic systems, and to the number and shifting positions of their various units. Matter is thus a structure of energy units revolving with immense velocities in Space-Time. The various elements arise from the number and arrangement of the units in an atom; and since these can be varied, the transmutation (the conversion of one element into another) of elements becomes possible, as it does in Radioactivity. The peculiar serial character of the Periodic Table of the elements is due to the number of units and their architecture in the atoms. Atomic Weights and Atomic Numbers reflect this inner arithmetical character of atoms.

The states of matter — gas, liquid and solid — are also the result of the residual surface forces of atoms and molecules, which are due in turn to their inner structure. Crystal structure is another result of inner atomic structure. But perhaps the most remarkable state of matter is one that combines the others; this is called the colloid state (a form of matter in which very fine particles of one substance are dispersed throughout another, like fat globules in milk). The colloid state is much more universal than is commonly thought, and it is especially important because the protoplasm (the living jelly-like material inside cells) of cells is organised in this state. The minute size of the dispersed particles means a maximum of surface area exposed compared to their mass. These surfaces bring surface forces into play and exhibit peculiar affinities or selective properties of various kinds, and as a result certain chemical and physical reactions are facilitated at these surfaces. This makes them useful in industry as well as in the processes of organic life. In fact, some reactions in the colloid state come strikingly close to the biological type.

From the analysis just given of the structural and energetic constitution of matter, certain conclusions can be drawn that very much narrow the gulf between matter and life.

First, the old view of matter as inert and passive disappears entirely. Matter, like life, is intensely active; the difference is not between deadness and activity, but between two different kinds of activity. Through their common activities, the fields of matter and of life therefore overlap and intermingle, and the absolute separation between them disappears.

Second, Radioactivity in matter plays a role somewhat analogous to that of Organic Descent (Smuts’s term for biological descent with modification — i.e., evolution by descent) in life. Both render fluid the old fixed entities and forms; though the difference between them must not be downplayed. In particular, we must recognise that Radioactivity is regressive (it works backwards from heavier elements to lighter ones), while Organic Descent is progressive. But this may be due to the extreme age of matter compared with the youthfulness of life in the history of the earth.

Third, the Periodic Table of Chemistry bears a distinct resemblance to the systems of classification used in Botany and Zoology; the concepts of family, genus and species could be applied to both. This shows that the characters of activity, plasticity, and probably of development and genetic relationships, apply to both the organic and the inorganic domains.

Fourth, the structural character of matter indicates that matter is also creative — not creative of its own stuff, but of the forms, arrangements and patterns that constitute all its value in the physical sphere. Just as life and mind are creative of values through the selective combinations and forms they bring about, so matter too — instead of being merely dispersive, diffuse and structureless — produces, through its own inner activities and forces, structural groupings and combinations that are valuable, not merely to humans, but in the order of the universe. Without its dynamic, structural, creative character, matter could not have been the mother of the universe.

Fifth, the discreteness and discontinuity in matter — emphasised in the quantum, the atom and so on — is the basis of the individual distinctness in life, which makes all living forms distinct individuals, of which no two are identical.

Sixth, matter in its colloid state in protoplasm reveals properties, and manufactures substances such as chlorophyll and haemoglobin, that are necessary for the functions of life and that go a long way towards bridging the great gap between the two. In its colloid state, then, we see matter reaching up to the very threshold of life. A great leap may have taken place across what remained as a gap. A great “mutation” (in the biological sense — a sudden, discontinuous change rather than a gradual variation) may have occurred. But since life probably began at a much lower level than the lowest forms we know today, the mutation may after all not have been so great. In any case, a close inspection of the nature of matter, as revealed by the New Physics and especially by colloid chemistry, brings it very close to the concept of life.

LET us now go on to consider how recent advances in our knowledge of the make-up of matter have emphasised this same feature of structure in the physical universe. Chemistry had for more than a century been exploring with great success the structure and constitution of matter, but the New Physics of Radioactivity has, in the last generation, proved a most powerful aid to Chemistry and led to discoveries little short of revolutionary. Chemistry is responsible for the analysis of matter into a certain number of elements, each with its own physical and chemical properties; for the discovery of the atom as the ultimate unit of each element of matter; for showing how atoms of an element unite into simple molecules of that element, and how atoms of different elements unite into compound molecules. The combination of elements in definite quantitative proportions was explained as the union of one or more atoms of those elements with one another. From this it might be inferred that chemical combinations are like arithmetical combinations, and that the whole numbers of arithmetic might properly represent the atoms of chemistry and their combinations into compounds. This inference, however, has only really been verified by the recent physical discoveries. It was not just the fact of numerical or quantitative structure that was important to Chemistry; the spatial or positional structure of matter — the order in which the atoms are placed and spaced in a chemical substance, the architecture of matter — became almost equally important, and in many cases the properties of a substance could only be explained on the basis of its real or supposed inner structure and configuration. Thus molecules of carbon could be either coal, or graphite, or diamond, and this great dissimilarity in the same chemical substance was explained as the result of differences in the placing and spacing of the atoms in the carbon molecule. Sulphur and many other elements show a similar polymorphous (existing in several different solid forms), or as it is also called, allotropic (existing in two or more forms in the same physical state) character. It was, however, when chemists had to explain the differing characters of quite distinct chemical compounds that nevertheless had the same chemical composition that the importance of “structure” and constitution became most highly accentuated. Such compounds are called isomers (compounds with the same chemical formula but different arrangements of atoms, and therefore different properties). So important is structure to matter that without it, one may safely say, organic chemistry becomes unintelligible. The more complex the composition of substances (as in organic chemistry), the larger the number of permutations and combinations possible in the relative positions and placings of atoms or groups of atoms in the make-up of matter, the more important the phenomenon of isomerism becomes, and the greater the part played by structure and configuration in the building up of matter. The chemical formula is no longer sufficient — it is a mere abstract notational shorthand which can be thoroughly misleading in the absence of a diagrammatic representation of the constitution or structure of the compound. The crystal forms of solids illustrate not only the structural character of chemical substances, but also the invariable way in which the same substance follows the same pattern of structure. For Chemistry, structure — or the proper representation of the relative positions and linkages of atoms or groups of atoms in three-dimensional space — has become indispensable. And the New Physics has now gone a step further and shown that this minute structure of the chemical atom and compound is not static in space, but dynamic and intensely active in that four-dimensional Space-Time continuum which we have already found to be dominant in the relations of astronomical bodies and events. Space-Time prevails at both ends of physical infinity and everywhere in between.

For Chemistry, the atom was a hard, indivisible unit; its constitution (if it had any) could not be known. Chemistry could not explain chemical affinity, nor why atoms combined into molecules, nor the strange serial character of the Periodic Law in regard to atomic weights and the properties of atoms. These triumphs were reserved for the New Physics, which has traced structure back into the innermost recesses of the atom. The discovery of Radioactivity by Becquerel in 1896 was the first indication that the atom was not indivisible and could break up spontaneously in nature. The discovery the year before, in 1895, of the X-rays by Röntgen revealed for the first time the existence of invisible rays whose wavelength was as small as atoms; the spectrum of these rays has provided an instrument of incredible power and accuracy for investigating the almost infinitesimal phenomena of atomic structure. There followed in 1897 the isolation by Sir J. J. Thomson of the ultimate unit of negative electricity — the electron — and in 1900 Max Planck’s discovery of what came to be known as the quantum, the unit of action emitted by all radiating bodies (and even by dark bodies). The application of these new ideas and means of investigation by a number of brilliant researchers led to the elucidation of the nature and constitution of the atom in the theory specially associated with the names of Sir Ernest Rutherford and Professor Niels Bohr. Without going into details that don’t concern us, and simply to illustrate the element of “structure” in the atom that we are dealing with, I shall summarise the main points of this theory.[1] On this view, an atom is an electrical constellation somewhat like our gravitational solar system: at its centre is a tiny but very massive nucleus that is positively charged, around which equally tiny electrons (negatively charged particles of very small mass) revolve. The nuclei are so massive that, in the Hydrogen atom for instance, the nucleus has 1,835 times the mass of the electron. The electrons revolve at various rates in their different orbits, all of which can be measured through their X-ray spectrum; and an electron can suddenly and all at once jump from one orbit to another, increasing its orbit when it absorbs one or more quanta of radiation from some outside body, or decreasing its orbit and taking one nearer the central nucleus, in the act of doing so releasing one or more quanta of radiation. It is these quanta of radiation, released when an electron jumps to a narrower orbit, that account for the light coming from the sun and the stars and indeed from all radiating bodies; and it is the definite quanta of radiation so emitted that account for the peculiar spectrum of the elements in the spectroscope. Why atomic light should be emitted in these definite amounts or quanta is not yet known, but it is known that the quanta shown in the spectrum follow a scale somewhat similar to the notes in music; we may therefore think of light as the music of the spheres (an ancient Pythagorean image: the planets producing harmonious tones as they move), in which the total harmony or light effect is made up of definite, discontinuous notes rather than a continuous variation of light. The wonderful thing is that on all of these matters we have the most minute and accurate knowledge: the size of a quantum; the mass, velocity and orbits of an electron; the mass and rate of rotation of a nucleus; and the total volume of an atom, with its small nucleus and electrons and vast empty spaces, comparable to the empty spaces of our solar system. The electron is by now very well known, and indeed all electric currents are nothing but streams of free electrons. But of the corresponding positive unit — called the proton — next to nothing is known, since the proton has never yet been isolated. (Footnote: The Rutherford-Bohr theory has been largely superseded by the new quantum mechanics associated mostly with the names of Schrödinger, Heisenberg, and Dirac, which dispenses with all planetary or other pictures of atomic structure and behaviour, and consists of purely mathematical principles that explain atomic phenomena. The feature of structure is, however, just as markedly emphasised in the new theories as in the earlier Rutherford-Bohr symbolism.)

Now the nucleus of an atom may be simple or complex; it may be a single proton, as in the case of the Hydrogen atom, or it may consist of several protons — some of which may, in turn, be neutralised by closely associated electrons, while others remain un-neutralised, so that the nucleus as a whole always remains positively charged. In the Hydrogen atom there is one proton in the nucleus, and so one electron revolves round it. In the Helium atom there is a nucleus of four protons, two of which have electrons associated with them while two do not; the nucleus therefore has two unbalanced positive units, and there are two electrons revolving around it in the atom. The combination of four protons and two electrons in the Helium nucleus appears to persist in other nuclei, so that the nuclei of the other elements appear to be combinations of simple (Hydrogen-type) and complex (Helium-type) nuclei. The number of revolving electrons in an element always corresponds to the number of unbalanced positive units in its nucleus; this is called the atomic number of the element, and it is always a whole number. The atomic numbers of the elements thus run from 1 in the case of Hydrogen, to 2 in the case of Helium, to 6 in the case of Carbon, and so on, up to 92 in the case of Uranium, the heaviest of all the known elements. Of these 92 possible elements, two have not yet been isolated, although their atomic numbers and weights, their positions in the Periodic Table, and their approximate properties are known. Atomic weights are in every case whole numbers; the apparent exceptions are cases involving isotopes (atoms of the same element whose nuclei contain different numbers of neutral particles, giving them slightly different weights but the same chemical behaviour). Thus, incidentally, the New Physics has explained the mystery of the Periodic Table.

The mystery of the atom has now largely narrowed down to the nucleus, which consists of an inner revolving system of protons. Comparatively little is known of these except that they rotate round their centre with an enormous velocity — probably not much less than that of light — and that the quantum law and the mass law of Relativity both apply to them. As space or volume contracts with velocity in Space-Time, the mass of the nucleus increases at high speeds out of all proportion to its size. The positive nucleus of the atom is therefore effectively where the atom’s mass resides, with the rest of the atom being either empty space or very light, insubstantial electrons. Owing to its sheer massiveness, the nucleus of protons is therefore coming to be identified with matter itself — as if matter were ultimately just high-speed, densely concentrated positive electricity. The proton may therefore yet prove to be the fundamental unit of matter. The significance of this view is that it reduces matter simply to a form of energy, or rather of Action (Smuts uses this physics term as roughly: energy times time, the basic quantity that the quantum measures), and so simplifies the scheme of the universe still further.

There is another fact that shows the intimate relation between energy on the one hand, and structure or mass on the other. The mass or atomic weight of the free Hydrogen atom has been determined as 1.0077. As we have seen, in the Helium nucleus there are four protons or Hydrogen nuclei, but here their mass appears as only one. In other words, free Hydrogen atoms or protons (which are practically identical as regards mass) suffer a reduction of mass when they are concentrated into the Helium nucleus — as if, in this nucleus (which is itself an inner constellation system), the protons and electrons are so close that they jam against each other, move more slowly, and thereby decrease their mass or matter. Energy of motion (kinetic energy) has become the lesser energy of position, or potential energy. When the Helium nucleus is split up again into Hydrogen protons, this loss of mass or potential energy would be recoverable in the form of kinetic energy. Small as this energy is in the Helium nucleus alone, it must be enormous in the world at large, since in all matter the nuclei are composed either of Hydrogen protons or Helium protons (their compressed form), or both. Should this energy ever become economically available, the greatest potential source of energy in the universe will be opened up — for the benefit, or destruction, of mankind.

Such a process would involve the artificial breaking up of matter, and this is the very phenomenon we already witness in a natural, spontaneous form in Radioactivity. In Radioactivity, the nuclei of the heavier elements (Uranium, Thorium, and Radium) spontaneously break up and eject Helium at an unvaryingly slow rate — regular enough to serve as a geological clock, which is now being used to calculate the age of the oldest rock formations of the earth.[3]

The Periodic Table shows that the expulsion of three Helium atoms from Uranium will convert it, by way of Thorium, into Radium; the expulsion of one more Helium atom will convert Radium into an element called Radium Emanation; and so on, until eight Helium atoms have been expelled and Lead is reached. If the process of expulsion could be continued, Mercury would be reached next, and after that, Gold. The alchemists, then, were not so far off the mark when they guessed that Mercury could be transmuted into Gold! Unfortunately (or, as a citizen of the greatest gold-producing country, I should rather say fortunately), this spontaneous breakup of matter has not yet been observed to proceed beyond Lead. The artificial breakup of matter in the laboratory began with the experiments of Sir Ernest Rutherford, who, by bombarding Nitrogen gas with alpha particles from Radium C, succeeded in splitting the Nitrogen atom into Hydrogen atoms and a residual apparent combination of Helium nuclei — which might result in Carbon according to the Periodic Table, but which is more likely to split up into Helium atoms. This artificial destruction of the elements has now been extended to other cases. Whether it will ever become economically feasible is a question for the future to answer.

We have seen that the free positive charges of the nucleus must be balanced by an equal number of negative electrons, grouped in their orbits around the nucleus. The number and grouping of these planetary electrons determines the external physical and chemical properties of the atom. If the orbits the electrons follow place a strain on the equilibrium of the atom, a quantum adjustment to a different orbit will be made. If the number of electrons and their orbital distribution produce complete equilibrium, the atom will be very stable internally and inert or inactive externally; it will belong to one of the inert groups (Helium, Argon, Neon, Krypton). On either side of this inert group in the Periodic Table we find elements whose atoms have one electron too many or too few; in other words, they are not internally in equilibrium and have a negative or positive charge unsatisfied. They will therefore combine with any other element that has an opposite charge unsatisfied. A further step away from the inert elements in the Periodic Table brings us to elements with two negative or positive charges unsatisfied, which will combine with another element having two opposite charges unsatisfied. And so on, to the elements with three, four or five unsatisfied charges. In this way both chemical affinity and the valency of the elements (mono-valency, di-valency, etc. — valency being the number of chemical bonds an atom can form, equal to the number of unbalanced charges it has) are accounted for. In every case, the external properties of the element are simply the expression of its internal structure and of its condition of stable or unstable equilibrium with respect to its inner elements.

Not only the combination of atoms into molecules, but the formation of the most complex compounds, rests on this condition of unstable equilibrium due to unsatisfied negative or positive charges in the combining elements. The compound, instead of being a single solar-type system, is a far more complex affair: it represents a case in which suns with their attendant planets in turn revolve round a greater central sun, or in which several solar systems are linked together externally rather than around a common centre. In either case, the distribution and equilibrium of the moving internal electrical units determine the structure of the substance as matter, and also its physical and chemical properties; while the movements of the substance as a whole, and of its parts relative to each other, create the gravitational field or curved Space-Time system that forms the medium and the field of the substance. There is thus structure through and through — not only in matter (or in the energies that, in their extreme concentration and velocity, take on the massive form of matter), but also in the field that surrounds it.

The gaseous, liquid and solid forms of matter are also the result of this inner condition of electrical stability in the atom and molecule. If the positive and negative charges are quite equal and properly distributed, the result, as we have seen, is an inert element. Such an element will also be a gas, since the inner satisfaction of the charges and the balance of the system make it inactive or inert externally. All gases are states of matter in which the inner balance of equilibrium in the atoms and molecules is such that there is no residual force to act externally; the atoms (in inert elements) and molecules (in others) therefore move freely and unhampered. If the inner balance of charges is not quite complete, there will be some external residual force between the molecules, and the liquid state results. If this inner satisfaction is reduced still further, the resulting external strain among the molecules will increase, they will attract each other still more strongly and tend to closer aggregation, and the solid form appears. The negative or positive electrical condition of the gas, liquid or solid will be an indicator of the still unsatisfied charges residing in the substance in that state. The free, unhampered movement of atoms in an inert gaseous element, and of molecules in other gases, makes the question of any particular shape immaterial; gases as gases have no particular form. With liquids, however, the resultant residual forces of the atoms and molecules will, as is the case in electrically charged bodies, act mostly at the surface, where the resulting force between the molecules of the surface layer — the surface tension, as it is called — will give a particular form or shape to the liquid (as in a drop of water). The molecules inside a liquid appear to be stratified into layers loosely superimposed on one another. In the case of solids, the still larger residual force results in arranging the molecules into a definite crystal structure on the pattern of a lattice, which is the special and specific form of solid chemical substances. Crystal structure is to solid compounds what planetary structure is to the atom — not only a specific ordering of inner units, but the index and source of all external properties and activities. One of the most interesting recent discoveries is that in crystals there is a unit body or minute structure consisting of two or more molecules, which is of a radicle character (acting as a single unit, like a chemical radical — a group of atoms that travels and reacts together as one) in that it always acts as a unit in the building up of the crystal.

Besides the gaseous, liquid and solid phases of matter just discussed, there is a fourth, to which an ever-increasing amount of attention has been and is being devoted in recent years. This is the colloid state, in which one substance is dispersed throughout another in very minute particles — particles that are nevertheless larger than molecules. Originally, substances were divided into colloids and non-colloids; but more recently it has been shown that non-colloids (such as mineral salts) can, under certain conditions, be reduced to the colloid state. So this division has now been abandoned, and the colloid state is recognised as a fourth form of material aggregation, applying to substances generally under certain conditions. Much of the earth and the air exists in the colloid state. But the colloid state is especially important because it seems to be distinctive of all life-forms — the protoplasm of all organic cells being organised in the colloid state. The protoplasm of cells contains solid substances in extremely minute form dispersed throughout its jelly-like fluid, and this colloid state seems to be what links the inorganic with the organic substances in the cell.

Owing to their minute size, particles in the colloid state expose the maximum surface area in proportion to their mass. The colloid state therefore brings the play of surface energies into action more than any other phase of matter. In all forms of aggregation the surface molecules of matter are specially orientated, with the active sides of the molecules turned inwards and the outer surface consisting of the weak ends of these molecules. This orientation affects the surface tensions, chemical behaviour and energies of the surface molecules; and as colloids expose a maximum of such surfaces, they show properties of a distinctive kind. At these surfaces, loose, unstable combinations with other special substances are easily formed, and so colloids appear to have a peculiar and almost mysterious selective action for other substances. The phenomenon is called “adsorption” (the adhesion of molecules of one substance to the surface of another, distinct from absorption, in which one substance is taken up into the bulk of another); the selected substances are said to be adsorbed at the colloid surfaces. Colloids are accordingly used in many industrial processes to separate substances from one another, to remove impurities, and otherwise to act as a selective separator of mixed substances. They also act as catalysts — that is, at their surfaces, chemical actions take place and combinations are effected that would not otherwise come about. The colloid surface is, apparently, a special field of force or influence in which other chemical or physical reactions, besides selective adsorption, are facilitated. The enzymes in the protoplasm of the cell, for example, are complex chemical substances in very minute colloid form, with their surface molecules or radicles specially orientated so as to facilitate, in a most marvellous way, the chemical and physical processes necessary for the activities of life. But enzymes are very particular or specific in their action, and each particular process has its own particular enzyme to bring it about. Thus enzymes transform the sugar or sugar-like contents of certain plants into alcohol; but each species of plant has its own enzyme, which will only operate on the material of that species. Likewise, chlorophyll is probably a complex chemical compound in colloidal dispersion in the protoplasm of leaf cells and other green cells; its colloidal surfaces are “fields” in which the energy of sunlight can synthesise the carbon dioxide of the air into organic compounds, which ultimately take the form of sugar, starch and cellulose.

The marvellous behaviour of matter at its surfaces in the colloid state — and especially its mysterious “selective” power — has raised the hope that it is here that the bridge may yet be found between the inorganic and the organic. As Dr. E. F. Armstrong puts it: “Enough has been said to show how the conception of an orientated active structure at the surface of a colloid aggregate might endow it with selective power of so fine a nature as almost to merit the description of intelligence; the further prosecution of research on these lines may well serve to bridge the gap between us and the full understanding of vital activity.”[4]

It has been usual to distinguish “physical” from “chemical” combination. The New Physics has, however, made it clear that there are really two types of chemical change, involving two types of chemical combination and structure. The first, which prevails among the salts, acids and bases of inorganic chemistry, is a much looser and less rigid combination or union than the second, which prevails among the carbon compounds of organic chemistry. Common salt, for example, which is a combination of sodium and chlorine, is now understood to be a more or less loose aggregation of free positive sodium atoms (or ions) held in equilibrium by an equal number of free negative chlorine atoms (or ions); the equilibrium is fairly stable, but without any actual union of the atoms such as the older chemistry assumed. In organic compounds, by contrast, the linkage between the constituent atoms is real, and the compound is not a system of free ions in equilibrium but a real combination or fixed structure of the atoms concerned. Organic compounds therefore display an advance in chemical structure over inorganic substances. While in inorganic salts and similar substances the looser arrangement of the atoms or ions approaches the type of “physical” combination, in organic substances the chemical union is more thorough and more intense, and leads to a closer structural character, with the atoms linked together by common electrons. In this connection, it is important to remember that organic compounds are the mechanisms of life: we may therefore say that as we approach life, we witness a more intense element of structure in chemical compounds. Life may have arisen in — or at any rate now uses as its mechanisms — chemical substances of a subtler structure than that which characterises inorganic compounds.

In connection with the explanation of the structure of the atom given above, the question arises whether the structure of the atom is really as described, or whether we are merely dealing with a hypothesis used to explain certain facts. The question is important, because it raises one instance of the general method of scientific explanation. Science deals with sensible (i.e. perceptible — capable of being detected by the senses, even via instruments) phenomena and tries to co-ordinate them in accordance with known physical laws, and in doing so often has to interpret the sensible phenomena in a particular way in order to bring about the necessary intelligible co-ordination. In the case of the atom, its existence as a fact is no longer disputed. But its structure modelled on a planetary system was no more than an inference from well-grounded sensible phenomena, and has now been virtually abandoned in favour of a system of mathematical equations as a better symbolism. The so-called “structure” may be no more than a hypothetical symbolism. The sensible phenomena are quite different from the inferred structure, but they are quite definite, and have been most minutely measured or calculated. The electron and the nucleus have not been observed, but certain light effects, which they accurately express, have been observed, and from these effects their mass and other properties have been calculated. The sensible phenomena actually observed include light effects, which are explained on the hypothesis that they are transmitted in particular wavelengths; these explanations match the observed effects, and they in turn form the basis of the supposed velocities, rotations and orbits of the electrons and nuclei — which are not directly observed but calculated, with extraordinary minuteness and accuracy, on the basis of the observed light effects. Likewise, the light from the atom comes in definite, observed quantities, which it has so far only been possible to interpret intelligibly as sudden changes in the orbits of rotating electrons. The observed phenomena are light effects of various definite qualities and quantities; the rest is theory or hypothesis, in which the elements of quality and quantity in the sensible phenomena are so minutely analysed and translated into elements of time and space as to result in the structure of the atom given above. This hypothetical structure is then tested by all the phenomena that call for explanation, and it is finally accepted only when it provides a complete explanation of them all. The electrons, the nucleus, the revolutions of the electrons round the nucleus, the sudden leaps of electrons from one orbit to another — these are not observed realities or sensible phenomena, but they all rest on a basis of sensible light effects, which have been meticulously determined and tested by reference to other observed phenomena. They are therefore not sensible realities but accepted scientific realities. They are not directly observed, but deduced from observations. They are the reflection, so to say, of the sensible phenomena in the human mind with its particular conceptual equipment. And if they are not the actual forms of nature, they are so close to them, and measure and represent them so completely, that they are accepted as true and correct — that is, in experimental accord with the deliverances of our senses — until they are upset by new observations and replaced by new hypotheses. Thus the apparently unrelated and unintelligible data of sense in a particular case are, by hypothesis, construed into the structure of the atom; and the atoms with all their inner units and arrangements become the conceptual or scientific entities that correspond to, reproduce, and represent the data of sense. In other words, the conceptual or scientific order arises on the basis of the sensible observed order, and so long as the two are in working accord we accept them both together as the explanation of nature. While giving complete respect to both orders, we should always bear in mind that the sensible order is the governing factor, to which the conceptual order has to conform. As long as it does so conform, we accept the conceptual order — not as sensible reality, but as an accurate measure, expression, and completion of sensible reality. The hypothetical structure of the atom reproduces and expresses the observed facts; without such structure the observed facts are unintelligible and inexplicable. We therefore accept the structure as a true and accurate explanation of the observed facts, even though it has not been directly observed as a structure. But the structure is really no more than a hypothesis, to be discarded as soon as it comes into conflict with new facts.

I close this chapter with a few general reflections on the nature of matter, which will serve to emphasise and interpret the results of the foregoing discussion.

As I indicated in the first chapter, the aim of this work is to make a modest contribution towards reforming the fundamental concepts of matter, life and mind — to help bridge the apparently impassable gaps between them and to interpret them in such a way as to present them as successive, more or less continuous, forms and phases of one great Process, or as related progressive elements within one total coherent reality. In pursuit of that general aim, my purpose in this chapter is to pave the way for a reform of the concept of matter — to break down the old concept of matter as something inert, passive, barren, dead; as something with absolute outlines and nothing beyond; as something final, presenting an impassable barrier to the kingdoms of life and mind beyond. This cannot be done by general philosophical reflections on the nature of matter as an object of thought, but only by a careful consideration of the concept of matter in the light of all the available physical knowledge. That must be my excuse for having referred at some length to the Theory of Relativity and the New Physics. Certain general results emerge from our discussion that have an important bearing on the concept of matter.

First, the old concept of matter as dead, passive and inert is clearly inconsistent with the recent developments of physical science. The old contradictory notion of dead matter as the vehicle and carrier of life must disappear in the light of our new knowledge. The difference between matter and life is no longer measured by the distance between deadness or absolute passivity on the one hand, and activity on the other — a distance so great as to constitute an impassable gulf in thought. The difference between them is merely a difference in the character of their activities. Far from matter being pure inertia or passivity, it is in reality a mass of seething, palpitating energies and activities. Its very dead-weight simply means the push of its inner activities. Its inertia, which is apparently its most distinctive quality and which Newton enshrined in his First Law, has received its death-blow at the hands of Einstein. From the new point of view, the inertia of matter is the same as its mass, and both are simply the result of the movement of nature’s internal energies; matter’s apparent passivity is but the external aspect of its real activity. Matter itself is nothing but concentrated structural energy — energy stereotyped (set firm, fixed in pattern) into structure. As space contracts with velocity, so mass or the inertia of matter increases through that contraction; both the mass of matter and its quality of inertia or passivity are therefore mere variable, dependent aspects of nature’s high-speed energies. From this point of view, matter is just a form of energy, concentrated by its exceeding velocity and structured to appear massive or substantial. The very nature of the physical universe is activity, or Action. The Law of the Quantum rules all.

The repercussion of all this on the old concept of matter is deadly. Once the new point of view is thoroughly grasped and absorbed into popular thought, the bugbear (the imagined source of fear) of matter ceases to trouble our peace. We no longer keep staring at a hopeless, irreconcilable contradiction in experience. With the dissolution of the old traditional concept of matter, the dead-weight of its utter passivity disappears from people’s minds, and one of the greatest partition walls in knowledge falls down. Establishing the contacts with life may still be very difficult. But, at any rate, the impassable gulf has disappeared. With the contours of the old fortress of matter razed, its very field points the way for the transition to the kingdom of life beyond. The fields of matter and life overlap, intermingle, and interpenetrate one another; the fruitful contacts are established; and the enriched, broadened concepts of matter and life appear as what they truly are — different phases in the evolution of an essential unity. The breakdown of the old concept of matter has prepared the way for a great advance towards a new synthetic world-conception.

Second, another advance of the New Physics has perhaps even greater significance in bringing matter and life closer together. I am referring to the effect of Radioactivity in destroying the permanence of the natural elements, and in explaining the genesis of the elements from one another. Radioactivity has done somewhat similar work for matter as Darwin’s theory of Organic Descent did for life two generations ago. The fixity of the types of matter has followed the fixity of the types of life into the limbo of obsolete ideas. Of course, there are marked differences between the operation of Radioactivity and that of Organic Descent. In one respect, Radioactivity has not proved as powerful a factor as Organic Descent, since it holds out no promise of the creation of new species or elements beyond those already known. The Periodic Table does indeed indicate the vacant places for one or two more guests yet to arrive. But the number of elements is definitely and narrowly limited, and we have at present no firm reason to look forward to any large increase beyond those already known.[5]

[Footnote] Professor Millikan, however, speculates that “cosmic rays” are due to the disintegration in distant nebular universes of higher, more complex elements than are known to us, and that the debris of this disintegration is then redintegrated (recombined or built up again from its parts) into known elements in interstellar space, so that matter is recreated. This recreation of matter is, however, thought to be inconsistent with the second law of Energy (the second law of thermodynamics, which says that disorder — entropy — always tends to increase in a closed system). The question, though, is whether the second law applies in the intra-atomic world and in interstellar space.

In another important respect, Radioactivity differs from Organic Descent. Organic Descent claims to show how new and future species arise through variation and selection from those already in existence. Radioactivity operates in the opposite direction: it shows how, by the elimination of certain unit constituents from a complex element, there can be a regress to another, simpler, already-known element. In the time-series, Organic Descent claims to move forward, while the process of Radioactivity appears to move backward, retracing evolutionary steps taken in the past. In yet another respect, Radioactivity appears to be even more effective than Organic Descent, since it exhibits before our eyes the process of the transmutation of elements, while it has not yet been definitely established that any natural species has been raised in the laboratory — or will ever be raised in any period short of geological time. In a final respect, there is a striking similarity between the two factors, in that both appear to proceed by definite substantial increments or decrements when bringing about transmutations. Radioactivity expels definite numbers of Helium nuclei as steps in the transmutation of elements. According to De Vries and others, the process of advance from old to new species or varieties proceeds by way of definite, discontinuous mutations, and not by the slow summation of minute variations. Today, Genetics emphasises this similarity still more, by identifying all organic variations with differences of genes in the nuclei of varying or mutating species.

The differences just noted in the operation of the two factors of Radioactivity and Organic Descent arise partly, no doubt, from inherent differences between matter and life, but also partly from other possible differences in their circumstances of a less fundamental kind. Life is a mere child on this globe and is still in the heyday of its growth and increase. As yet, in its first flush of youth, it recognises no limit or barrier. It spends with a lavish prodigality (extravagant generosity) that is in striking contrast to the frugality and conservatism of matter, for which the laws of Conservation (which says total energy or mass-energy is preserved) and of Least Action (the principle that the path actually taken by a physical system is the one that minimises a quantity called the action) have become the last word of wisdom and the unbroken rule of action. But matter is old — old as the beginning, so old that its wrinkles are the fundamental curves of the Space-Time universe. Life has only just begun, since the yesterday of Eozoic times (the earliest geological eon in which life is supposed to have existed, well over a billion years ago), to build up its new forms and types, and in this task it can carry on for millions of years to come. Matter, by contrast, completed its active race probably more than a thousand million years before life began. Slowly and laboriously, in nebular cold and solar heat — amid conditions beyond the possibility of our knowledge or imagination — it built up the elements from their simplest to their most complex forms, and from these elements substances and compounds of rising complexity, until at last protoplasm was reached. In the favouring bosom of protoplasm, life could be nurtured from its simple chemical beginnings and launched on its great career, most of which is still ahead of it. The work of matter, apparently, is done; in the great Space-Time curve, it is now regressing from the more complex to the simpler types or elements — just as in organic Evolution we see a tendency for the most highly evolved and differentiated types to revert, for stability, to simpler and stronger types. Radioactivity is doing today what Organic Descent (when it has truly become a descent in the regressive sense) will do in the fullness of its time, when life’s spirit of adventure has abated and its aim becomes safety first — conservation rather than progress.

When all allowance has been made for the differences in character and operation between Radioactivity and Organic Descent, there still remains a striking and unmistakable similarity between them. And between the Periodic Table of Chemistry on the one hand, and Systematic Botany and Zoology on the other, there remains something very much like a family resemblance. The concepts of family, genus and species could be applied to both. In both cases there is a fluidity and plasticity of types which proves that, although they belong to different kingdoms, they are yet in the same world of forms and geneses. One rises from a study of the Periodic Table and the New Physics with the feeling that matter can quite justifiably claim some distant relationship with life, and that life need not be ashamed of the rock from which she was hewn (a biblical phrase, Isaiah 51:1, meaning the source from which one comes).

The intimate character of structure that the material universe and its field disclose justifies another general observation as it bears on the concept of matter. We have already seen that, properly understood, the ideas of activity, plasticity and evolution apply to matter in a sense not entirely unlike the sense in which they apply to life. I am going to make a more daring suggestion now, and indicate that in another, even more important respect, matter approximates to life. The structure of matter indicates that matter is also, in a sense, creative — creative, that is, not of its own stuff, but of the forms, arrangements and patterns that constitute all its value in the physical sphere. It is creative in a sense analogous to that in which we call life or mind creative of values. Remember that, on the new point of view, we are not to judge of matter from the outside, as indifferent external spectators. We have to identify ourselves with the point of view of matter, so to speak. We have humbly to get into that closed cage; we have to take our post on that flat circular rotating disc.[6]

We have to interpret matter from the inside, from a point of view that is matter’s own and not remote from or indifferent to it. From that intimate angle, matter is seen to create its structures and patterns and values very much as life or mind does on another, much higher plane. Hitherto, the idea of creativeness has been confined to the organic and mental aspects of the universe. Those who have called the universe creative have implicitly meant the activity of life and mind in creating new arrangements, meanings and values. It has not been suggested that, from another point of view, the physical universe too is creative. The principles of the conservation of matter and energy have effectively barred any such idea. Novelty, originativeness and creativeness are quite inconsistent with the ordinary point of view and the popular ideas of matter, as well as with the more rigid mechanistic conceptions of science. No one, however, could have followed the foregoing exposition of the structural character of matter without beginning to appreciate that, in its evolution or creation of the forms, structures and types that characterise it from beginning to end, matter — the physical element in the universe — is in a sense as truly creative as is the organism or the mind. The “values” of matter or of the physical universe arise purely from these structures and forms. If the stuff of matter, or energy, or action, were not definitely structural but diffuse throughout space, the entropy (the measure of disorder or unavailable energy) of the universe would be absolute, and its value, from any point of view, would be nil. The efficiency, utility and beauty — in short, the values of matter — arise from the structures that are the outcome and expression of its own inherent activities. In a very real sense, the idea of value applies as truly and effectively in the domain of the physical as in that of the biological or the psychical. In both cases, value is a quality of the forms and combinations brought about. Whether they are structures resulting from the activities of matter, or works of art or genius resulting from the activities of the mind, makes no real difference to the application of the ideas of creativeness and value in either case. We have to take “value” not merely in the human order, but in the order of the universe. A setting sun is part of nature, and its beauty is part and parcel of the order of the universe. Once we get rid of the notion of the world as consisting of dead matter into which activity has been introduced from some external or alien source; once we come to look on matter not only as active, but as self-active — active with its own activities — our whole conception of the physical order is revolutionised, and the great barriers between the physical and the organic begin to shrink and shrivel. The organism has, by its inner activities and the influences of the environment, evolved its own forms and types, and this great life-process is still going on before our eyes. As I have already suggested, a similar evolution of material structures and elemental types may have gone on during the practically infinite period of past time. And it may even be that, although new elements will no more be evolved, derived structures are still being created under suitable conditions. It is interesting to note, for instance, that under novel laboratory conditions new substances are continually being synthetically produced. The whole romance of the aniline dyes (a class of synthetic dyes derived from coal-tar chemistry and discovered in 1856 by William Perkin, the first major triumph of synthetic organic chemistry) is a tribute to the still active “creativeness” of matter under the proper external conditions.

There is another feature common to matter and life that points to a genetic relationship between them. That feature is particulateness, discontinuity, discreteness, in both material and living things. The search for continuity has been one of the most persistent quests of science — the reduction of differences, the resolution of complexes, and the smoothing out of curves, so that the way might be made smooth for that ideal of monism (the philosophical view that all reality is ultimately one) which haunts the visions of science. This search for continuity has had wonderful success: the vast multiplicity of different things in the material world has been reduced to a few ultimate entities and a few fundamental laws. And yet, the deeper science has delved, the more fundamental this element of discreteness in the nature of things has become. The flow of nature is interrupted by halts and pauses in all directions. The atom was such a halt, and now that the atom has, in a sense, joined the flow of things, the quantum has appeared as an even more serious stop. Nature stores or expends her energy not continuously, but in fixed quantities or quanta — in spurts, not in a continuous jet. If there were not these mysterious halts, she might run down in a flash, as Jeans has pointed out. Discreteness is thus discovered at the very sources of nature. It is this discreteness, this individual element, that especially distinguishes organic nature, even more than the inorganic. All forms of life are individual and different from each other; there is no identity anywhere. All attempts to smooth out the curves and unevennesses of evolution and produce the semblance of a continuous advance have failed. Mutations — discontinuous jumps — seem to be the law of advance. And the discontinuity seems to be entrenched in the ultimate genes or factorial elements in the germ-cells. As we go on with our enquiry, we shall have to stress more and more this element of discreteness, of individuation or individuality in Evolution. Here we find its real basis laid in the ultimate energy unit, in the quantum.

These considerations, in so far as they have any force, must influence our concept of matter and tend to reduce the utter disparity that has marked our traditional concepts of matter and life. Of course, a great difference remains between these two concepts — between the chemical compound on the one side and the organic cell on the other. It would be futile to try to argue away this difference. It is, and remains, great; but its character has been fundamentally transformed. We may put the conclusion of our discussion this way. In organic Evolution we come across mutations — not absolute breaks with the past, but sudden long steps of advance on the past, where one species or variety leaps forward from and beyond another. In the advance from matter to life, there is a leap forward, not as between species, but as between kingdoms. So we may conclude by saying that, instead of the old impassable gulf between matter and life — between the chemical compound and the cell — we have found, on closer scrutiny, only a mutation: the greatest mutation of all, undoubtedly, in the whole range of science, but essentially nothing more than a mutation. They show the faint lineaments of a family resemblance, and as science advances and our philosophy looks more deeply, the resemblance will become clearer and more unmistakable.

Lastly, we have seen that matter in its colloidal state reveals properties and shows behaviour which seem in some ways to anticipate the processes and activities of life in its most primitive forms. In any case, it begins to lay the basis of those physical and chemical reactions which are specially required for vital (life-related) activities. It shows a certain power of selectiveness, which may be related to chemical affinity, but which seems to have a further reach and to partake of the character of life. It begins to manufacture substances such as the proteins, chlorophyll, and haemoglobin — the special mechanisms of life, without which life as we know it could not be. These substances are the links connecting material structure with the life-structures that are to follow in the course of Evolution. They are themselves non-living chemical substances, but they are the special instruments and the very basis of life, so to say. At their colloidal surfaces, the energies of nature are used to convert the inorganic material of nature into the most complex organic substances required for the sustenance of life. We see matter, in this colloidal state, reaching up to the very threshold of life, so to speak. A gap remains; a great leap may have been made across it. But beyond a doubt, some forms of matter in their colloidal state come fairly close to life in their properties. And beyond all doubt, life began with much more primitive forms and structures than any of which we have knowledge today. So the gap may not have been so wide, nor the leap so great, as it would appear to us today.
