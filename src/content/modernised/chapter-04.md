Summary: The cell is the second fundamental structure in the universe. It is possible that, both before and after the origin of atoms and cells — as well as in between — other structures arose in the course of cosmic Evolution. If they did, they have passed away, and we now have only these two enduring survivors that we can examine for clues to the basic character of the universe. In the study of animate (living) nature, Evolution or Organic Descent (Smuts’s term for descent with modification, i.e. evolution by descent) has until recently attracted most of the attention. But more recently the study of the structure and functions of the cell has come rapidly to the forefront, and now probably forms the principal centre of interest in Biology. That all plants and animals consist of cells; that cells contain certain peculiar bodies called nuclei; that all higher organisms arise from cell-fusions in which the nuclei play a prominent part — all these facts have been discovered only in comparatively recent years, and our knowledge of cells is therefore still in its earliest stage. But Cytology (the scientific study of cells) is now, with much-improved research techniques, making rapid strides, and great discoveries are confidently anticipated.

Besides the nucleus, the cell consists principally of a rapidly circulating jelly-like fluid, enclosed in a more or less well-defined wall or membrane that is permeable. This fluid contains many extremely complex chemical compounds in solution or in the colloid state (a state in which very fine particles of one substance are dispersed throughout another). The structure of a cell is therefore highly complex, and in fact comparatively little is yet definitely known about it. Its functions are even more mysterious, since they include practically all the activities we see in developed organisms — birth, growth, breathing, feeding, digestion, self-healing, reproduction and death. Its most distinctive function is metabolism, which means that it thoroughly alters and transforms all food materials before assimilating them; all its apparently physical activities are of this transformative metabolic character, rather than being simple mechanical operations. The cell appears to form complex chemical compounds, called enzymes, which in their colloid state enable these distinctive radical transformations to be effected. Apparently simple physical processes such as osmosis (the diffusion of a solvent, like water, through a semi-permeable membrane) in the cell are really far more complicated, since they are brought about through enzyme action — a physico-chemical mechanism distinctive of organisms. Laboratory attempts to repeat organic processes therefore throw little light on the exact nature of these processes.

The origin of the cell is the origin of life, and is still a profound mystery. However, the reproduction of cells seems to admit us into the inner secrets of life. The cell-divisions that precede cell-fusions in reproduction bear an extraordinary resemblance to electrical situations, and seem somehow to connect the electrical structure of the atom with a possible electrical origin of the cell. It is now, however, impossible to follow up this striking resemblance further, since the original electrical processes (if there were any) have probably been overlaid by other developments that have transformed them.

Judging from the way sunlight acts in the growth of plants, it is not unlikely that the cell of life arose when the sun was both warmer and richer in chemically active rays, and when the waters of the earth still contained many substances in solution and dispersed in colloid form. The adhesion of cells to one another would account for the origin and development of multicellular organisms; and the divisions of cells, which we now see in growth and reproduction, may have arisen originally from the breakdown of cells or groups that had become too complex to be stable.

The reproduction of plants and animals — including the preparatory reduction division (the cell-division now called meiosis, which halves the number of chromosomes so that the resulting reproductive cells can later combine to make a new cell with the full set) of the sexual cells — follows largely the same plan. It is therefore probable that this wonderful organic mechanism was evolved before the split of life into plant and animal forms took place, and that it dates back to the early beginnings of life on this globe. The plant type arose from its dependence on air and earth for food, which was consistent with a fixed position; while animals, which need organic foods, required mobility, and therefore developed a motor (movement) system, with a nervous system to operate it, and ultimately a brain to co-ordinate and control both.

The cell differs from the atom or molecule in its far greater complexity of structure and function, in the differentiation and specialisation of its parts and organs, and in the system of co-operation among all its parts which makes them function for the whole. This co-operative system exists not only within the single cell but also among the multitudinous cells of organisms. The system of organic regulation and co-ordination among an indefinitely large number of parts — which makes them all function together for certain purposes — is a great advance on the system of physical equilibrium found in atoms and compounds; yet it is still quite distinct from the control which, at a later stage of Evolution, Mind comes to exercise in animals and humans. Mind as we know it should therefore not be ascribed to the cell or to the lower organisms; but organic regulation seems, on that lower level, to be even more effective than Mind is at a later stage.

This organic regulation and synthesis of functions is seen not only in all the ordinary functions of organisms, but most especially in their capacity for self-restoration in cases of mutilation. In such phenomena, something more than just the parts seems to be actually at work; the parts appear to play a common role and to carry out some common purpose, or to act for the common wellbeing. They seem to respond to some central pressure. There seems to be a central regulator. We have already seen a factor in matter making for structure; we now see a factor in organisms making for the central regulation and co-ordination of all parts. We are evidently in the presence of some inner factor in Evolution that requires identification and description. That will be attempted in the next chapter.

THE atom and the cell are the two fundamental structures in the universe that we currently know of — the atom being the unit of the world of matter, and the cell the unit of the world of life. In the last chapter we considered the structure of the atom and showed how the external properties of the atom were the expression and resultant of its internal energies and their structural grouping inside the atom. We saw the atom as a little complex world of its own, underlying both the atom’s outward properties and the field of that little world. We now move on to consider the vastly more complicated little world of the cell and its field. In the science of life, the two most significant conceptions are Evolution and the Cell — the one being the unit structure of life, and the other the general character and trend of the activities or functions of life. Most of the progress and interest in biological science since the middle of the nineteenth century has centred on the investigation and development of these two governing conceptions; the results so far obtained have been of the highest importance, and practically revolutionary for our entire world-view. And the end is by no means in sight yet. In the first chapter we saw that there are still deep-seated misunderstandings of the nature of Evolution, and that a proper appreciation of Evolution would require a reshaping not only of biological concepts but also, and above all, of our concept of matter. Let us now turn to the cell, as the other and no doubt the real governing factor of the situation of life, and see what light it throws on the nature and concept of life.

A few introductory words about the history of our knowledge of the cell may not be out of place here. It will become evident that even the accurate information we have about the little we do know of the cell is of very recent date, and that we are only at the beginning of what may yet prove to be a great story.

In the second half of the seventeenth century, Robert Hooke observed with the crude microscope then available that cork and other vegetable substances had a vesicular (small-cavity-like) appearance, and he called the apparent cavities “cells.” A few years later, Grew and Malpighi independently observed in plant tissues these same cavities filled with fluid and surrounded by firm walls, as well as what appeared to them to be tubes, also with walls and filled with fluid. Towards the end of the eighteenth century, Treviranus showed that these tubes were really cells placed in a row, elongated in the direction of the row, with the partitions between them lost. Then followed, in 1831, Robert Brown’s great discovery of the nucleus in the plant cell, and in 1838 Schleiden’s elucidation of the great part the cell with its nucleus plays in the structure of plants; shortly afterwards, Schwann applied this new knowledge of the cell to the structure of animals as well. Both Schleiden and Schwann attached great importance to the cell wall, and looked upon cells as having crystallised out of some mother substance. Schleiden vaguely called the contents of cells “vegetable slime,” and it was not until about the middle of the nineteenth century that the great German biologist von Mohl correctly explained the contents of both vegetable and animal cells as nucleated masses of what he called “protoplasm” (the living jelly-like substance that fills cells) — a substance that was not a chemical crystallisation out of other substances, but always came into being as the offspring or daughter cells of pre-existing cells. From this came the formula: omnis cellula e cellula (Latin: “every cell from a cell”). This paved the way to the correct understanding of sexual fertilisation as the union of two cells; the discovery of cell-divisions and of the part played in them by the nucleus with its chromosomes (the rod-like structures inside the nucleus that carry the hereditary material); and the discovery of the origin of embryos through repeated cell-divisions. Finally, a concentrated effort was made by many investigators in many countries to discover the physical mechanism of heredity in cell-divisions and fusions, and especially in the part played by the nucleus. During this present century, the rediscovery of Mendelism by De Vries and others, and the rise of the new science of Genetics, have led to redoubled efforts to find the explanation of the many peculiar phenomena of heredity in an analysis of the parts played by the nucleus and the other elements in the protoplasm of the cell. At present, experimental Cytology is being vigorously pursued with numerous improved methods and techniques.

Let us now consider the structure of the cell and the part it plays in organisms. I shall only summarise its most general and outstanding features, with a view to illustrating the considerations and speculations to be advanced later. I am trying to find concepts for vital (life-related) phenomena that will be coherent not only with those phenomena, but also with broader aspects of knowledge and reality, and so a reference to the scientific facts and findings is necessary. The time is past when a philosophy of life could be developed without knowledge of, or reference to, the scientific facts and viewpoints.

All plants and animals consist of cells, and these cells are themselves usually composed of various chemical substances, some of which have a very complex constitution. The number of cells in an organism varies according to its size and complexity. Some of the lowest, most primitive organisms are unicellular (made of a single cell) or composed of comparatively few cells, while at the other extreme the higher plants and animals may contain untold millions of cells. The human brain alone is estimated to have about 9,000 million cells! These cells, again, are of the most diverse character: the cells that build up the various parts and organs of the body differ from one another. Thus the cells of nerves, bones and muscles — indeed of all the parts of the animal organism — differ markedly from each other, and the number of different kinds of cells that go into the make-up of a body may be indefinitely large. All these almost innumerable cells, of all kinds and degrees of differentiation and complexity, are arranged in a stable, orderly structure in the plant or animal body. And this structure is not stationary; like its constituent cells, it is in continual movement and development. The structural order which we have seen to characterise the inorganic element or compound is even more characteristic of the vastly more complex organic body, with its continuous mobility and transformations.

A plant or animal can be considered from the point of view either of its structure or of its functions — that is, of the activities performed by the structure as a whole, or by the parts of which it is composed. Viewing it merely as a structure, we see the same orderly combination and arrangement of parts as in the inorganic body — only the constituent parts and the structural arrangements are far more complex than in the inorganic body. In water, for instance, or in any other chemical compound, all molecules are more or less the same, and the body consists simply of a repetition of the fundamental molecule; the structures in which the molecules are arranged are likewise of a repetitive character. In an organic body, by contrast, there may be an indefinite number and variety of cells, and the variety of arrangements and structures by which these cells are combined in the several parts and organs of the body may also be indefinite in number.

But the difference between inorganic and organic bodies lies not only in their structures, but even more in their functions — especially the functions of the organic cells, to which there is apparently nothing corresponding in the inorganic world. About these cells we currently know comparatively little, except that their functions and activities are the basis of the functions and activities of the organisms they compose, with all of these being co-ordinated into a single system of a new type called “life.” In the march of Evolution from the inorganic to the organic, the cell is the real innovation, with nothing corresponding to it yet discovered in the inorganic. To use a metaphor: the cell is the point at which matter or energy roused itself from its slumbers and became active from within — with activities and functions that reveal its inner character and nature, so to speak. It is a new kind of structure, in which energy develops or acquires a new form of activity, becomes functional, and is somehow inexplicably endowed with special characters of selectiveness and reproduction, of self-help and self-control, which constitute a unique departure in the universe.

Let us briefly summarise some of what is known of the structure and functions of the cell. Since the plant cell is simpler than the animal cell, let us take that as the type. It consists of chemically very complex substances called collectively protoplasm, which is the physico-chemical basis of all forms of life. Comparatively little is known of its composition or chemical structure. The plant cell (less so the animal cell) secretes a containing wall or membrane for itself — from which the cell takes its name. Inside the wall, the protoplasm appears as a jelly-like fluid, consisting principally of a small nucleus (which contains certain chromatin bodies of a rich protein character — chromatin being the stainable substance from which the chromosomes are formed) and a larger body of cytoplasm (the protoplasm outside the nucleus) surrounding the nucleus and reticular in structure — that is, consisting of a network of spaces that contain various cell-saps and solutions, and even minute particles of crystals and other inorganic bodies. The whole constitutes a colloidal system, as we saw in the last chapter. The cell walls are semi-permeable, allowing the osmosis through them of certain substances and not of others, so that suitable food and other substances can be passed through the cell walls from one cell to another. There is a constant circulation and agitation of the cell fluid, which gives it the appearance of a stream, and which is much more than the random Brownian movement (the random jiggling of small particles suspended in a fluid, caused by their being struck by the molecules of the fluid) found in inorganic colloidal mixtures. The movement of protoplasm, whether it is Brownian or something different, has much more of the character of definite specific direction; and this is probably just an expression of that selectiveness and directiveness that are inherent and universal characteristics of all life-forms. Although little is definitely known about the details of cell structure, the functions the cell performs are so many-sided, delicate and complex that one may safely say that the cell must have an immensely complex organisation, and that the details of its constitution may never be fully known or even adequately pictured by the human mind. At one end of the scale of existence, the cell represents a minute, detailed complexity that is in some sense comparable to the wonders of the astronomical universe at the other end. And all this intricate, complex little system is maintained in a state of active, moving equilibrium; it is dynamic through and through, and incessantly active in all its details, and its almost innumerable activities are finely adjusted to one another and co-ordinated into a harmonious process — a process that not only maintains its balanced functioning for the cell’s individual life, but also increases and improves it across innumerable generations. Looking at this baffling mystery of active, continually changing and developing organisation, with its continuous delicate adjustments of innumerable moving parts into one co-ordinated forward movement, we find that the ordinary physical categories of description fail us. We feel ourselves in the presence of an entirely new phenomenon, which we call life, and we may even feel tempted to go further and to say that the cell has not only life but also mind. To do so would, however, be going too far, as I shall explain later.

To appreciate the position more fully, let us look at some of the functions of the cell. It is very difficult to grasp — yet it is the fact — that the little microscopic or ultramicroscopic cell probably does all or most of what the plant or animal as a whole is known to do. It literally breathes or respires; it takes in, manipulates, digests, and assimilates its food; it reproduces its kind; it grows, decays and dies; it heals itself when sick and restores itself when a breakage occurs. It develops special means and mechanisms to assist it in carrying out these operations, and it co-ordinates and regulates all its manifold activities in a way that implies some wonderful central control over all these functions. Let us look at these operations in a little more detail.

Unlike any other substance in nature, the protoplasm of the cell is vitally active and is in an incessant process of real, creative change; parts are continually being destroyed and replaced by new protoplasm that is continually being formed. No other substance has this power of making its own material, so to speak. A crystal, for instance, builds itself up from its own material already existing in solution, without any change being made in its constitution. The crystal merely serves as an attractive centre around which its material, already present in dissolved form, can be deposited in solid form. With protoplasm, the process of growth or renewal is quite different. The material taken in is entirely altered and recombined into the substance of which the protoplasm is composed; this material, so altered and transformed, is then taken up and assimilated, by some still-unknown process, into the protoplasm or living substance of the cell. This complete transformation, and this mysterious assimilation of its material, is one of the most distinctive functions of the cell, and its far-reaching significance will be more particularly stressed later on.

The technical name for this complete transformation that the cell effects in the material it takes in is metabolism; and one may therefore say that metabolism is the process which, above all else, distinguishes living from non-living matter. The cell is not a static or stationary organism. It is for ever being built up by new material that it transforms into its substances, and it is for ever being broken down through the new cell substances it secretes and gives off in order to build up the various parts of the complete plant, or to supply the energy needed for its functioning. And the activity by which material is taken in in one form, then transformed and assimilated into the substance of the cell, and then again given off as different cell substances for the building up of the various parts of the plant or for the energy supply — this activity, while apparently a series of chemical and physical processes, implies a co-ordinated system unlike anything seen in the purely physical or chemical domain. The physical and chemical procedure seems to be merely the mechanism or instrumentality used by a deeper organic process — a process that means and does much more than the physical or chemical details we can identify. Not only is there control and organisation of these details, but the physico-chemical agencies themselves are of a new type. Oxygen and carbon dioxide, for instance, appear to be taken in through the stomata (the pores in the leaves) of the leaves, but this is not merely a case of ordinary osmosis. Liquid materials in the form of dissolved salts or other inorganic substances are taken in through the roots, but this is not a case of ordinary physical osmosis either. These liquids rise in the plant cells as if it were merely a case of surface tension or capillary action. But as a matter of fact these are all cases of metabolism, in which subtle changes take place in the protoplasm — changes whose details no doubt all appear to be of a physico-chemical character, but whose distinctive character lies not so much in those details as in the new system of control by which they are organised and regulated. The theory developed to account for the physico-chemical reactions that take place in all organic change and functioning is based on the assumption of very complex substances of the nature of ferments or enzymes being formed and acting in the protoplasm. It is, for instance, through the agency of the enzymes in the protoplasm that all the secretions are formed which build up the different parts of the plant. The transformation of carbon dioxide in the green cells of the leaves into starch, for example, is not a chemical change of the ordinary type; it is effected in the presence of colloidal catalysts (substances that speed up chemical reactions without themselves being consumed) like chlorophyll and other enzymes, at whose surfaces sunlight can transform the carbon dioxide so as to form, successively, formaldehyde, dextrose, maltose, and finally soluble and insoluble starch. Mere physical and chemical reactions have been identified. But it is quite possible that there is much more, and that the organic process behind them is much more complicated and characteristic. Both respiration and metabolism are processes effected through enzyme action at colloid surfaces, rather than being of the ordinary mechanical character. The enzymes are thus conceived as catalytic agents existing in colloidal form in the cells, and as having at their surfaces or in their “fields” the power of transforming other substances in the presence of the energy of sunlight or electricity. They do this according to the well-known chemical and physical laws, without themselves being used up or transformed in the process. The cell has the power to build up or secrete these complex enzyme compounds; with their help, it then manufactures other complex substances necessary for plant or animal life. It carries on many other functions in addition to these manufacturing processes. Throughout, it seems to follow simple physical and chemical rules, but on a new plan. All this serves to emphasise how vastly complicated cell structures and activities must be. A large number of the most complicated processes are carried on — scarcely one of which the best-equipped laboratory in the world can perform — and all are carried on by a little cell that is microscopic or smaller in size!

In recent years, resolute attempts have been made to reproduce, under artificial laboratory conditions, what takes place in the living plant, and certain very interesting results have been obtained. Professor Baly and others, for instance, have tried to imitate photosynthesis in the laboratory. Light of a short wavelength from a mercury vapour lamp was made to act on water and carbon dioxide, and as a result, formaldehyde was obtained — and, as in the green leaf, oxygen was set free: CO₂ + H₂O = CH₂O + O₂. Light of a somewhat longer wavelength was then made to turn this formaldehyde into simple sugars. More recently, Professor Baly has succeeded in synthesising formaldehyde and sugar from carbon dioxide and moisture by the action of sunlight on the surface of certain coloured inorganic compounds, such as nickel carbonate, which take the place of the coloured chlorophyll in the natural process. However interesting and valuable these and similar results are, it is probable, if not certain, that they bear only a distant resemblance to what takes place in the organic process — in which the physical factors of sunlight and electrical change, acting in the field of colloid chlorophyll, are different, and the chemical changes may be different too. From a scientific point of view, however, the laboratory work is undoubtedly interesting and important, since the detailed reactions in organisms are undoubtedly physical and chemical. In living organisms there is, however, over and above these particular reactions, a specific synthesis (a binding-together) of them all, which constitutes the real essence of life. And this synthesis has an influence over the detailed processes.

The origin of the cell is the origin of life, and we know nothing definite about it. But the question arises whether enough is now known about the cell and about organic development to justify our trying to form some general idea of its possible origin. Here we find one set of phenomena that throws a special light on the nature and development of organisms, and perhaps also on their origins. The phenomena of reproduction seem to hold the very secret of life, and what is more, they bring us close to the secret of matter. This common secret — jealously guarded and preserved throughout the whole range of terrestrial evolution — shows a continuity unique in science, which brings together some of the apparently most diverse facts that confront us in the world of matter and of life. How well the secret has been guarded, kept and shielded from all outside influences is shown by the extraordinary fact that, although plant and animal life must have diverged near the very beginning of life, and must have been moving further apart through many hundreds of millions of years of the history of this globe, the methods of reproduction in plants and animals are still very much alike. The romance of the reproduction of a flowering plant, which is one of the most wonderful in the world, is practically the same, down to many details, as the reproduction of one of the higher animals. Going very far back, we find that it is very much the same in some of the simplest, most primitive algae as in the other members of the rising plant series — the ferns, the cycads, the conifers, and the flowering plants. When we go still further back into the past and come to the case of unicellular organisms, which reproduce themselves not by cell-fusions but by cell-division, we come to the situation, or something very close to the situation, that must have arisen when matter first organised itself into life. For what do we see? When the cell proceeds to divide into two, it takes on the appearance of an electrical and polar system. Its nuclear material arranges itself in parallel bodies or chromosomes, like an electrical or electromagnetic field; its centrosome (a small body in the cell that organises the apparatus of division), if any, splits up as if under some unbearable electrical strain, just as is the case in Radioactivity, and two polar bodies are formed from it on opposite sides of the nucleus, from which lines of force proceed throughout the now-disintegrating nucleus and cell; the nuclear bodies of the breaking-up cell divide themselves equally between the two polar bodies, and aggregate and concentrate towards them until finally the separation between the two systems is complete and the material of the nucleus and cell has split into two. The division of the cell into two cells is complete. It is plain from this brief sketch how the dividing cell approximates to the character of the atom of matter described in the last chapter. Were they not, in the beginning, both electrical systems with their nuclei, their fields and their cataclysmic behaviour? In the cell, the original hypothetical electrical character of the division has become overlaid with, and obscured by, other factors, so that the electrical character is no longer recognisable except in the general appearance and scheme of division. But originally it possibly was electrical, as it still is in appearance. Reasoning back from the analogy of cell-division to the probable original rise of the cell out of inorganic matter, we may imagine the building up of very complex organic or hydrocarbon compounds under favourable external conditions, in which the influence of sunlight and other forms of electrical energy played an important part — just as sunlight in the presence of chlorophyll still plays a foremost part in the production of new cells and organic substances in plant life. We know that a thousand million years ago, when life arose, the sun was much hotter than it is today, and sunlight contained much more of the chemically active rays that facilitate organic change. The peculiar electrical energy of the sun may therefore have played a decisive part in the origin of life. In other words, the part that electrical changes appear to play in the process of cell-division may be somewhat analogous to the part they probably played in the original rise of the cell. The basic electrical structure of matter would thus be paralleled by the more complex electrical origin of the cell. Reproduction in the most primitive forms takes place in a fluid medium, and all protoplasm still has a fluid jelly-like consistency. It is therefore probable that the most primitive forms of protoplasm may have arisen under favourable conditions of sunlight and warmth, when warm water still contained much of the earth’s crust in solution or dispersed as small particles in colloidal form, and so presented conditions favourable for the selective formation of complex substances, such as the predecessors of the present forms of protoplasm. Recent advances in biochemistry have led to the isolation and discovery in the animal body of very complex substances (such as glutathione) whose external surfaces absorb free oxygen, and whose interior undergoes the opposite change of setting free this oxygen and building up higher organic structures. A continual chemical process is set going, which is at the same time an electrical current from outside inwards, transmitting the electrical energy of sunlight contained in the oxygen of the air to the interior cell substances — where the oxygen is deprived of its energy and set free, and where, with this energy, complex compounds are built up which then store the energy required for the nutrition and other functions of the living organism. The chemical-electrical system that forms the fundamental mechanism of life, in the last analysis, simply uses the energy of sunlight, stored in the free oxygen of the air, to build the body of life from it; and there is thus the closest connection between sunlight and life-structures. It is in that connection, without a doubt, that the origin of life must be sought. Once the cell-structure had been evolved out of pre-cell structure — probably by way of prolonged trial and error or “natural selection” extending over long periods of time — its multiplication or reproduction would take place through the part played by electrical tension. The complex unstable electrical structure or organic substance, whose internal equilibrium would pass through various crises and changes in its “development,” would finally tend to break up, and under certain conditions to divide. This original haphazard division would gradually become stabilised and standardised, so to speak, until cell-division becomes the regular basis not only of all growth, but also of all reproductive processes, in both plant and animal.

At first, there could have been no essential difference between the growth and the reproduction of cells. By division, one cell was formed from another, and might either remain in association with the old cell, as is the case in all multicellular organisms (giving growth), or it might separate from the old cell and develop on its own as an independent organism (giving reproduction). This simple division still remains the process of growth in all organisms without distinction, and it remains the process of reproduction in all unicellular organisms and in the lowest forms of multicellular organisms. In less primitive, more developed multicellular organisms, however, the process of reproduction has become more complex, and has shifted to the union or fusion of two specialised cells or gametes (the reproductive cells — sperm-cells and egg-cells) to form a new cell. In such cases, another scheme — involving a double set of divisions — has taken the place of the simple division. While one of these divisions simply halves the total contents of the old cell quantitatively between the two new cells, the other, the reduction division, separates out the individual chromosome elements in the contents qualitatively, so that each of the two new cells receives half of these elements. This qualitative halving is necessary to prevent the continual and cumulative doubling of cell elements with the repeated reproduction of the same type of organism, and to keep the chromosome contents of the cells of similar organisms constant. The two cells or gametes, thus reduced in all respects to half of what they were before reduction, then unite to form the new cell, which once again has the full complement of chromosome elements. This reduction division in reproduction is common to both plants and animals above the most undeveloped types, and so we seem to have some justification for the most remarkable conclusion that this phase of reproduction must have developed before the separation of plant and animal forms took place. It also forms the basis of that alternation of generations (the regular alternation, in many plants and lower animals, between a generation having half the chromosome number and one having the full chromosome number) which is one of the most remarkable phenomena of life. Thus all organisms reproduced through cell-fusion have a generation in which the cells have the single or haploid (containing one set of chromosomes) contents — produced after the reduction division — and another generation in which the cells have the double or diploid (containing two paired sets of chromosomes) contents, present before the reduction division. In the higher forms of plants and animals, the generation of single-content cells — the gametophyte (the haploid generation that produces gametes) generation, as it is called — has been reduced to a very subordinate role and a short life, since it covers the brief period of the gametes or conjugating cells (the ripe sperm-cells and ova) in flowering plants and in the more developed animals. The double-content-cell generation — the sporophyte (the diploid generation that produces spores) generation, as it is called — has become dominant, and appears as the developed plant or animal that we see in nature today. But in some divisions of plants the gametophyte generation is still of some prominence. Thus the moss plant is the gametophyte generation, while the sporophyte generation appears as a subordinate parasitic form. And in the ferns, where the sporophyte is the dominant form, the gametophyte appears as a distinct plant which is in some cases a perennial. Its relation to the fern as another phase of it was unknown until about the middle of the nineteenth century.

This generation of the single-content or haploid cell — the gametophyte in the developed plants and animals of today — is interesting because it is probably just another illustration of the well-known principle that ontogeny repeats phylogeny (the principle, due to Haeckel, that the development of an individual organism re-traces, in its early embryonic stages, the evolutionary history of its species). That is, the history of the individual organism recapitulates, in its earlier or embryonic stages of development, the various phases of development through which its types of ancestor have evolved in the past. The gametophyte generation is thus a reminder of that earlier, simpler, more primitive phase in plant evolution, when the more complex sporophyte generation, which is dominant today, had not yet been evolved through cell-fusion in reproduction. Another interesting deduction has also been made. The gametophyte reproduces in a liquid medium, and in this and other ways carries us back to the time when life on this globe was still more or less aquatic, and the later land forms of the sporophyte had not yet arisen. It is even possible that the form of the present gametophyte may throw light on the particular line of descent of plant forms. The gametophyte of the fern, for example, is a flat thallus-like (resembling a thallus — the simple, undifferentiated body of an alga or fungus, with no true root, stem or leaf) plant, not unlike an alga. So it is quite possible that this form of the fern may give the clue to the fern’s origin from some alga-like progenitor in the far-distant past. May we not say that the prothallus (the small, leafless plant body that develops from a fern spore and bears the fern’s gametes) of the fern appears to connect the alga and the fern, and so to bridge the most widely separated epochs of the past in the evolution of plant forms?

From speculations as to the origin of the cell, we move on to consider the differentiations that have taken place among cells generally, and the particular differentiation of cells that has led to the divergence between plant and animal forms. It is commonly thought that animal forms are a later development and an advance on the earlier plant forms. This idea is largely due to the fact that in the animal there has been the special development of the new factor of mind, which, rapidly rising in the higher animals, has reached its highest level in the human species. But although animal forms may have developed further and come to attain to much higher levels than the plant forms, the question of origins stands on a different footing. The evidence points rather to a common origin, and to the earliest cells of life having been common to both plants and animals. The lowest forms of cell-life are even now practically indistinguishable into plant and animal. It is probable that this common phase, prior to the differentiation into plant and animal forms, lasted a very long time, and was marked by considerable advances in the development of these common cells — especially in view of the probable fact, already noted, that sexual reproduction of a fairly advanced type had perhaps been reached before the bifurcation took place and plants and animals were launched on their separate careers. What advance in cell development had been reached before this bifurcation, it is impossible to say, since only the very lowest unicellular organisms of a common character still survive, and the geological record has no evidence to give. Differentiation in cells must have begun as soon as daughter cells started to adhere to the parent cell and multicellular organisms were formed. In the unicellular Pleurococcus, which is about the simplest plant form known — noticed as the green slime on the damp bark of trees or wooden posts — we see the beginnings of this process of cell aggregation: daughter cells adhere to the parent cell until several divisions have taken place, and only then do they separate into individual cells. The Pleurococcus cell is globular, but during this attachment the cells are flattened at the surfaces of contact. In multicellular organisms, a layer of cuticle covers the outer cell walls in contact with the air, retarding the loss of water. Step by step, other differentiations appear, and the plant body becomes more complex as we advance from alga to fern, and from fern to the higher, seed-bearing plants. The differentiations into various organs — such as root, stem, leaf and reproductive organs — are simply means towards the division of physiological labour. In cells away from the light, photosynthesis is impossible, so they become dependent on the outer green cells; similarly, the roots underground become dependent for starch on the green cells, and in return absorb dissolved salts to supply the rest of the plant. Water requirements make the fibrovascular cell system (the network of conducting tissues, made up of water-conducting and food-conducting cells, that runs through the plant) necessary, while the reproductive functions become confined to special organs. All this differentiation means more organisation and a more elaborate structure of the plant. In addition to this division of labour, the struggle for existence tests the structure in other directions, and brings about more modification in response to the stress of the struggle and the stimulus of the environment generally. As a result, the plant’s structure comes to be elaborated and adapted to the inner and external demands placed on it, and assumes the forms known to us.

Besides this general differentiation and organisation, there are special causes that have brought about the divergence of plant and animal forms. A consideration of these matters falls outside the scope of our task. Generally, it may be said that plant life has been determined and stereotyped (set firmly in fixed patterns) through the two processes of photosynthesis and osmosis. Osmosis has enabled the plant to obtain water and mineral salts directly from the soil; photosynthesis has enabled it, with the help of chlorophyll, to use the energy of sunlight to make sugars, starch and cellulose from the carbon dioxide of the air. The plant, being thus dependent for its food solely on the soil and the air, could afford to remain stationary and mostly fixed in the soil. Animal forms, on the other hand, which depend on organic foods, have had to be mobile in order to look for and find the necessary plant or animal substances on which to live. The struggle for food has been a much harder one for animals, which have in consequence not only had to be mobile and to develop a complex motor system, but also to evolve, in the nervous system, a special co-ordinating mechanism with which to operate the motor system. This mechanism, in turn, has led to unique developments in the direction of sensitiveness and consciousness, which in the case of man have come to overshadow all that has gone before. But mind is a later development, and the discussion of it should not be raised in connection with the cell. The primitive cell of life is on the way to Mind, but Mind in any proper sense of the word is at this stage still far off, and those who ascribe Mind — or even potential Mind — to the cell open the door to the most serious confusions. The cell undoubtedly presents a great mystery. There is a strong temptation to ascribe its surprising activities to an inner mentality or organic psychism (the doctrine that something like mind or psyche is present even in the lowest organisms or in cells). But even the most highly evolved human intelligence finds it difficult to understand all that goes on in the cell. If psychism is the key, we should have to ascribe to the cell so large a measure of mentality as to reduce the whole supposition of psychism to absurdity. The cell does not yet have mind. Mind as we know it, or anything at all resembling it, is a much later development in the process of organic Evolution, as will be shown in Chapter IX.

Enough has been said about the structure and the functions of the cell to give a rough general idea of what the cell is. Let us now move on to consider the inter-relations of elements within the cell, and among cells in the same organism, and especially the aspect of co-ordination both within and among cells. In the first place, I ask: are the cell, and the cells in an organism, a co-operative system, in which the parts and their functions are so ordered and arranged that they co-operate for common purposes, and do not merely serve the separate ends of the individual parts?

There can be no doubt about the answer. The whole meaning and significance of metabolism is that the activities of the cell are not self-centred or self-regarding. The cell functions for other cells and for the plant as a whole. One element in the cell functions for other elements and for the whole cell organism. The secretions formed in one cell are intended to build up other cells or to serve the plant as a whole. The fibrovascular cells carry liquid food from one part of the plant to others. The carbohydrates formed in the green cells are transmitted and stored as food for all the other cells; the woody substances secreted from them are intended to strengthen other cells and the plant as a whole against the forces of the environment; the aroma and bloom secreted from them are meant to render attractive and adorn other parts of the plant, with a view to the preservation of the plant species as a whole. Indeed, all the processes of metabolism go to prove that the plant is one vast co-operative system, in which the individual cells, in their continuous functions and labours, make their contribution to the common cause, and work so that other cells, or the plant itself, or the species to which it belongs, may live. The cell is a delicate problem not only of structure but also of inter-related functions, so co-adapted that a real whole is thereby constituted. The cell, in its normal structure and functions, is the very type of co-operative action.

So far, I believe we are still on firm ground in our description of cell activities, and the co-operative character of organic functioning will be generally admitted. Can we go further and characterise this co-operation more closely? What is the nature of this cell or organic co-operation? Is it spontaneous or controlled? And if controlled, is it controlled internally or externally? Let me put the question another way. Are the cells, and the organs they form in the same plant or animal, free and independent, so that the co-operation we observe in their functioning is just an accidental result of their individual, uncontrolled reactions and behaviour? Or is there some co-ordinating factor that influences the cells and their organs in some specific direction, and so co-ordinates and unifies their functions and produces the co-operation we observe? And if the cells are not independent agents in the make-up of the organism but are under some form of unifying influence or control, is this an external factor like Natural Selection, as commonly understood? Or is there some internal principle of co-ordination, the influence of which is felt by the different cells — in response to which they react, so that their functions proceed broadly along the lines of a plan or pattern given by the nature of the particular organism? In either case there would be co-operation on the basis of co-ordination; but in the one case there would be an external factor at work in this co-ordination, and in the other an internal factor. It will be seen that the issue raised here, as between the cells inside the organism, is analogous to the one Darwinism has raised as between separate organisms in their struggle for existence. The answer, so far as the struggle among organisms is concerned, will be discussed in Chapter VIII. The results reached there will probably apply also to the case of the cell or cells within an organism that is here at issue. The subject is not free from controversy, and in this chapter I wish to avoid controversy and simply to describe the facts in the ordinary language of metaphor, which I trust will not prove misleading. Looking at the facts in an unprejudiced way, and without bias in favour of any particular theory, one cannot help being struck by the way in which the cells in an organism not only co-operate, but co-operate in a specific direction — towards the fulfilment and maintenance of the type of the particular organism they constitute. At this stage we have to steer clear of all ideas of plan, purpose or teleology (the doctrine that nature has built-in goals or final causes towards which it is directed) in the organic procedure. But, even so, the impression is irresistible that cell activities are co-operative, that they are inherently or through selective development co-ordinated in a specific direction, and that the impress of the whole that forms the organism is clearly stamped on all the details. The case is utterly unlike that of molecules in a piece of mere matter, which are alike, which are repetitions of each other, and which can be added or subtracted or otherwise expressed arithmetically. The cells are different; they are differentiated in definite respects, and the totality of differentiations fits into a plan or scheme, the fulfilment of which constitutes the complete organism. There are no repetitions — there is uniqueness everywhere — and the various unique entities and their functions fit into one another, more or less, so as to produce an organic whole. This whole is not an artificial result of its parts; there is something more than the parts, and this something appears to stand in definite relation with them, influenced by them and again influencing them, and through this continuous interaction maintaining the moving equilibrium of structure and functions which is the organism.

Look, for instance, at the way in which organisms behave when some cells or organs necessary for their maintenance are removed or injured. It is well known that many plants and animals have the power of restitution in case of damage or mutilation. The newt forms a new leg in place of the severed limb. The plant supplies the place of the severed branch with another. The regeneration may be effected from different organs and by different organs. If the crystalline lens is removed from the eye of a Triton (a kind of newt), for instance, the iris will regenerate a new lens, although the lens and the iris in this case have been evolved from quite different parts. Numerous similar curious facts of restoration could be mentioned. The broken whole in organic nature restores itself, or is restored by the undamaged parts. The cells of the remaining parts perform the novel task of restoring the missing parts. The power to do this varies among different plants or animals, and varies also among the different parts of the same plant or animal. Generally, one may say that the more highly differentiated and specialised an organism or a cell is, the smaller is its plasticity, or the power of the remaining cells to restore the whole in case of injury or mutilation. But the fact that the power exists in numerous cases is proof that the cells, through reproduction, can not only build up the original organism according to its specific type, but also that, when this type is damaged, the remaining cells — or some of them — can restore it, and complete the whole again. The normal power of the cells to build up an organism in reproduction or embryological development according to type is one thing, and it is marvellous enough even if one looks on it as merely a case of inherited routine. But the abnormal power to do this in the very unusual case, so far removed from all idea of routine, where the type is accidentally broken down, is something different, and shows how effective the power of the organism as a whole is, and how strong is the tendency towards the whole even in the individual cells. In some subtle way the damage creates a need, and the need stimulates the remaining parts to perform the functions (quite new to them) of the damaged parts, or to restore them in whole or in part. The very nature of the cells is to function as parts of a whole, and when the whole is broken down, an unusual extra task automatically arises for them — to restore the breach — and their dormant powers are aroused to action. And this happens, so far as we can see, simply as a matter of interior economy and domestic regulation in the organism itself, and without previous education for the new role. The interaction between the organism and its cells is indeed most subtle and intimate; both seem to be active factors in the maintenance of the whole, and in the restoration of any parts that may be missing and necessary for the whole. So intimate is their interaction that it is almost impossible to say where the influence of the one ends and that of the other begins.

The aspect of co-ordination, or of the subordination of parts to the whole, is also most significantly illustrated by the phenomena of reproduction, which I have already referred to in another connection. Reproduction not only carries us back to the past and its riddles, but also forward to the future, and it is the reproductive system of organisms that we must scan most closely if we wish to understand this aspect of organic activities. For in reproduction the cell or the organism clearly appears to go beyond itself: its functions become transcendent, as far as it itself is concerned; its blind strivings and energies embrace objects and situations beyond itself. In reproduction, in fact, the cell or organism bears clear testimony to the fact that it is not itself alone, and that it is part of a larger situation of life, towards the fulfilment of which its most fundamental functions are directed. As an illustration of the co-ordinated inter-relations of parts and whole in an organism, nothing can therefore be more significant and important than the facts of organic reproduction. Here, more than anywhere else, the importance of the whole appears — not merely the immediate whole or individual organism, but also the transcendent whole, or the type, which has to be reproduced and maintained at all costs. Throughout the entire range of organic nature, one is impressed with the essential selflessness, the disregard of self, and the transcendence (going beyond, surpassing) of self in the reproductive process — a process that harnesses the individual to the needs of the race, exhausts its reserves of strength, and often costs it its life. On that process is stamped, as on the very heart of nature, the principle of sacrifice, of the subordination of the part to the whole, of the individual to the race or type.

The preceding analysis will have enabled us to realise that the plant or the animal is a whole consisting of millions of parts in the form of cells of all kinds, while the cells in turn are smaller wholes of indefinite complexity and marvellous activities. All these parts are co-ordinated and arranged down to the most minute details, and function with the most complete collaboration in support of one another and of the organism. The organism is indeed a little living community in which law and order reign, and in which every part collaborates with every other part and serves the common purposes of the community, as a rule with the most perfect regularity. It is this perfect community of functions, and unity of action, in a system consisting of innumerable parts and the most complex structural arrangements, that makes the organism such a striking type of a whole. We have seen structural order as the characteristic of inorganic matter; we now see active co-operation and unity of action superadded as the characteristic of the organism. We admire the order and co-operation of a beehive or a community of ants. In the organism, we see a more perfect order and a more wonderful co-operation, in a situation that is perhaps not much less complex than either. And just as the individual bee or ant lives its own life and is not lost in the joint venture of the hive or the nest, so the individual cell lives its own life, and specialises and perfects itself for its role in the organism, helping to form and to serve that organism without loss or sacrifice of its own identity. The organism embraces innumerable smaller organic units, whose identity is not swallowed up in it, but expressed by it, not suppressed. The large organism does not only mean the union and co-operative harmony of its smaller units, but as a rule also their more perfect individuation (the process by which something becomes a distinct, individual whole) and specialised development within the harmony of the whole. The plant or animal body is a social community — but a community that allows substantial development to its individual members. Its nature and structure are such that it can only perfect itself through the differentiation and development of the members that compose it. While this is so, while (as we have seen, and shall see more clearly in what follows) individuation is fundamental in nature, we have to recognise that intensive co-operation plays a no less important and fundamental part. An organism is fundamentally a society in which innumerable members co-operate in mutual help, in a spirit of the most effective, disinterested service and loyalty to one another. Co-operation and mutual help are written large on the face of nature. What is more, if cell structure and function can teach us anything, they are imprinted deep on the world of life — they are the very meaning and soul of nature. We may travel far through the realms of Evolution, but nowhere shall we find a more perfect co-operation, or a more beautiful illustration of mutual help of one part for another — and of all parts for the whole, as well as of the whole for all its parts — than in the little, insignificant cell, which seems to hold the very secret of the universe. Anticipating the language of later developments, we may say that in the cell there is implicit an ideal of harmonious co-operation, of unselfish mutual service, of loyalty and duty of each to all — such as in our later, more highly evolved human associations we can only aspire to and strive for.

When the marvellous and mysterious stable constellation of electrical units in the atom was achieved, a miracle was wrought that saved the world of matter from utter chaos and chance. But a far greater miracle was wrought when, from the atomic and the molecular order, there was evolved a still deeper and subtler order in the inner co-operative harmony of the cell. These two fundamental structures are the great enduring achievements in the course of Evolution before the advent of Mind. Although many other experiments were probably made before, in between, and around these successes, those experiments proved unstable; they were discarded and abandoned, and are now searched for in vain. We have to scrutinise these enduring peaks of achievement if we wish to understand the real nature of the Evolutionary process, and if we wish to form an idea of the nature of the ground in between these permanent structures — ground which has been washed away in the endless lapse of time. And when we find these two not utterly different, but expressions of a somewhat similar inner progressive tendency in nature; and when we find later, on the mental and spiritual levels of development, still clearer expressions of a similar tendency — we shall be justified in concluding that we are face to face with something real and true, of far-reaching importance: a vera causa (Latin: a “true cause” — a real, genuine cause whose existence in nature has been established) in nature. The impression becomes so strong that it is not so much a matter of speculation as a recognition of clear, simple facts before us. These permanent structures in nature have been, and are still being, patiently investigated for us by science. As I said in the last chapter, they show more than a faint family resemblance, and they enable us to recognise the unity that underlies them and to draw certain conclusions about the origin of this unity. They point to a universal pervading feature of nature. The evidence in favour of such a synthetic ordering feature or process has been accumulating in this and the two preceding chapters; it requires isolation, identification and exact formulation, if that is possible. And in the next chapter a preliminary attempt at such an identification and formulation will be made.
